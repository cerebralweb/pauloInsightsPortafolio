THE LEAST COMMON MULTIPLE THAT SAVED 1/24 OF A LAUNCH BUDGET

In this article ❧ Budget decisions rarely fail at the level of individual line items — they fail because someone forgot to ask what the parts add up to. When marketing funds are split across multiple channels, the question is not how much each allocation costs, but whether the sum of those allocations still leaves room for the unexpected. This problem demonstrates how the Least Common Multiple — a concept rooted in classical number theory — becomes the financial instrument that makes fractional budget control precise, auditable, and reusable as code.

The Problem

A startup founder allocated 1/3 of the product launch budget to digital advertising, 1/2 to influencer partnerships, and 1/8 to event sponsorships.

What fraction of the budget remained unspent after those three allocations?

  • a) 1/24
  • b) 1/12
  • c) 2/3
  • d) She spent it all
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1/24 ✅

Math Solution

The three allocations — 1/3, 1/2, and 1/8 — use different denominators, so they cannot be added directly. The Least Common Multiple of 3, 2, and 8 is 24, which becomes the shared unit. Once each fraction is expressed in twenty-fourths, the allocated shares sum to 23/24, leaving exactly 1/24 unallocated.

The LCM is not just a computational step — it is the smallest unit of measurement into which every budget slice fits evenly, making the remainder immediately visible.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the unallocated fraction is derived:

  1. Identify the denominators: The three fractions are 1/3, 1/2, and 1/8 — denominators are 3, 2, and 8.
  2. Find the LCM: Prime factorizations: 3 = 3¹, 2 = 2¹, 8 = 2³ → LCM = 2³ × 3 = 24.
  3. Convert advertising allocation: 1/3 = (1 × 8) / (3 × 8) = 8/24.
  4. Convert influencer allocation: 1/2 = (1 × 12) / (2 × 12) = 12/24.
  5. Convert sponsorship allocation: 1/8 = (1 × 3) / (8 × 3) = 3/24.
  6. Sum all allocations: 8/24 + 12/24 + 3/24 = 23/24.
  7. Subtract from whole budget: 1 − 23/24 = 24/24 − 23/24 = 1/24.
  8. Conclusion: After committing funds to all three channels, the founder has 1/24 of the launch budget left unallocated — answer a).

What Is the Least Common Denominator?

Before the LCM can be computed, two more foundational definitions need to be in place. A natural number is any positive counting number — 1, 2, 3, and so on. A prime number is a natural number greater than 1 that has no divisors other than 1 and itself. The primes are 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, and continue infinitely. Every other natural number greater than 1 is composite — meaning it can be expressed as a product of primes. These two definitions are not preliminary formalities; they are the load-bearing vocabulary on which the LCD rests. Without natural numbers, there is no counting structure. Without primes, there is no way to decompose denominators into their irreducible parts. And without that decomposition, the phrase "least common" has no precise meaning.

The Least Common Denominator (LCD) of two or more numbers is the smallest natural number that is evenly divisible by each of them. For example, the LCD of 4 and 6 is 12, because 12 is the smallest number into which both 4 and 6 divide without a remainder.

Prime factorization is the cleanest path to finding it. Break each number into its prime factors, take the highest power of every prime that appears, and multiply them together. For 4 (= 2²) and 6 (= 2 × 3), that gives 2² × 3 = 12. The LCD is not just an arithmetic convenience — it is the point where the prime structures of two numbers first overlap. Without the language of primes, there is no precise way to explain why that overlap lands exactly where it does.

Historical Background

The challenge of expressing different fractions over a shared unit is ancient. Euclid's Elements (c. 300 BCE) addressed it systematically in Book VII, the first of three books on number theory, where he treated divisibility, the greatest common divisor, and the least common multiple as foundational tools of arithmetic — and crucially, he did so in the language of natural numbers and their prime structure. The full text of Book VII is available at the Clark University digital edition: Euclid's Elements — Book VII.

The standard method for finding the LCM — decomposing each number into prime factors and taking the highest power of each — rests on the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. The theorem states that every natural number greater than 1 has a unique prime factorization; that uniqueness is precisely what guarantees the LCD is well-defined and minimal. A version of unique factorization was implicit in Euclid, but it was not rigorously stated and proved until Carl Friedrich Gauss formalized it in his Disquisitiones Arithmeticae in 1801. The Britannica entry on this theorem is here: Britannica — Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. In a budget context, the theorem guarantees something operationally important: every set of denominators has exactly one minimal common unit, and that unit is the LCM. There is no ambiguity, no smaller alternative, and no risk of over-counting.

Python Version

The script below generalizes the problem to any three fractional allocations — enter any numerators and denominators, and it returns the unallocated remainder in simplified form using Python's standard-library fractions module.

from fractions import Fraction

def unallocated_budget(alloc1, alloc2, alloc3):
    total_allocated = alloc1 + alloc2 + alloc3
    remaining = Fraction(1) - total_allocated
    return remaining

print("Budget Allocation Remainder Calculator")
print("Enter each budget allocation as a fraction (numerator and denominator).\n")

num1 = int(input("Advertising allocation — numerator: "))
den1 = int(input("Advertising allocation — denominator: "))
num2 = int(input("Influencer allocation — numerator: "))
den2 = int(input("Influencer allocation — denominator: "))
num3 = int(input("Sponsorship allocation — numerator: "))
den3 = int(input("Sponsorship allocation — denominator: "))

remaining = unallocated_budget(Fraction(num1, den1), Fraction(num2, den2), Fraction(num3, den3))

if remaining == 0:
    print("\nResult: The entire launch budget has been allocated — nothing remains.")
else:
    print(f"\nUnallocated launch budget remaining: {remaining}")

Run it in Windows Terminal by typing python, pasting the script, and entering each numerator and denominator when prompted — it returns the unallocated fraction automatically simplified to lowest terms.

# Budget Allocation Remainder Calculator

This skill takes three fractional budget allocations — each entered as a numerator and a denominator
— and determines what fraction of the total budget remains unallocated. It finds a common unit of
measurement across all three denominators using the Least Common Multiple, combines the allocated
shares, and subtracts the total from the whole. If the allocations consume the entire budget, it
says so explicitly. Otherwise, it returns the remaining fraction in its simplest form.

---

## Dependencies

- No external libraries required. The standard-library `fractions` module is optional
  and improves accuracy for complex inputs.

---

## Inputs

- **Advertising allocation**: A fraction representing the share of the launch budget
  committed to digital advertising. Entered as two whole numbers: the numerator and the denominator.
- **Influencer allocation**: A fraction representing the share committed to influencer
  partnerships. Entered as two whole numbers: the numerator and the denominator.
- **Sponsorship allocation**: A fraction representing the share committed to event
  sponsorships. Entered as two whole numbers: the numerator and the denominator.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Present the Context

Print a short header identifying the tool as a Budget Allocation Remainder Calculator. Prompt the
user to enter each allocation as a fraction by providing its numerator and denominator separately.
Use clear, business-friendly labels for each prompt (e.g., "Advertising allocation — numerator").

### Step 2 — Collect All Six Inputs

Ask for the numerator and denominator of each of the three allocations in sequence: advertising
first, then influencer partnerships, then event sponsorships. Accept only whole numbers for each
entry.

### Step 3 — Find the Common Unit of Measurement

Determine the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the three denominators. Do this by first finding the
LCM of the first two denominators, then finding the LCM of that result with the third denominator.
The LCM becomes the shared denominator — the finest unit into which all three fractions can be
expressed without remainder.

### Step 4 — Convert All Fractions to the Common Denominator

For each allocation, scale its numerator by the factor needed to express it over the common
denominator. Do this by dividing the common denominator by each fraction's original denominator
and multiplying the result by that fraction's numerator.

### Step 5 — Sum the Allocated Shares

Add the three scaled numerators together. This total represents the combined number of budget units
that have been committed across all three line items.

### Step 6 — Calculate the Remaining Units

Subtract the combined allocated numerator from the common denominator. The result is the number of
unallocated budget units remaining out of the total common-denominator units.

### Step 7 — Simplify the Remainder

Reduce the remaining fraction to its simplest form by dividing both the remaining numerator and the
common denominator by their Greatest Common Divisor (GCD).

### Step 8 — Deliver the Result

If the remaining numerator is zero, print a message stating that the entire launch budget has been
allocated and nothing remains. Otherwise, print the simplified remaining fraction with a clear,
friendly business label — for example: "Unallocated launch budget remaining: 1/24". Do not print
only the number.
