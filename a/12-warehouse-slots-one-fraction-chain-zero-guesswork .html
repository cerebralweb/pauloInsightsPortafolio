12 WAREHOUSE SLOTS, ONE FRACTION CHAIN, ZERO GUESSWORK

In this article ❧ Inventory managers rarely know everything at once. Often a single confirmed count — one sub-group, one category, one verified number — is the only anchor available, and the total must be reconstructed from it. This problem illustrates how a chain of fractional allocations can be reversed from a known partial count, and how Python and a Claude skill file turn that logic into a reusable operational tool.

The Problem

A logistics manager is tracking a shipment of product boxes stored across several warehouse slots. One third of the slots hold refrigerated goods. One sixth hold fragile goods. One third of the remaining slots hold oversize goods.

The manager confirms there are 2 slots with oversize goods. How many warehouse slots are there in total?

  • a) 24
  • b) 12
  • c) 8
  • d) 16
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) 12 ✅

Math Solution

Refrigerated and fragile slots together consume 1/3 + 1/6 = 1/2 of the total. The remaining half is then divided, with one third of that remainder going to oversize goods — giving oversize slots a share of 1/6 of the total. Since 2 oversize slots equal 1/6 of T, the total is T = 12.

The key move is treating the remainder as a new whole: one third of one half equals one sixth of the original total.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the total is derived:

  1. Refrigerated share: 1/3 of T
  2. Fragile share: 1/6 of T
  3. Combined assigned share: 1/3 + 1/6 = 2/6 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2
  4. Remainder after two groups: 1 − 1/2 = 1/2 of T
  5. Oversize share (one third of remainder): 1/2 × 1/3 = 1/6 of T
  6. Set equal to known count: T/6 = 2 → T = 12
  7. Conclusion: The warehouse holds 12 slots in total.

Historical Background

Chained fractional reasoning — taking a fraction of a fraction to find a share of a remainder — was a standard tool of medieval Islamic commercial arithmetic. Scholars working in the tradition of al-Khwarizmi in ninth-century Baghdad used it to partition inheritances and trade goods across multiple recipients, where each heir's share was defined as a fraction of what remained after prior distributions. The method was formalized in treatises on practical arithmetic (hisab) and traveled through Andalusia into Renaissance European accounting texts, eventually becoming a core technique in early probability and combinatorics.

Python Version

The script below solves the same problem for any set of fractions and any known sub-group count, making it reusable for any inventory allocation scenario with the same structure.

# What this does: finds the total number of warehouse slots from one known sub-group count.
# Where it came from: a logistics problem where slots are split by fractions and a remainder.
# The math idea: fractions of a whole with a nested remainder, solved in reverse.
# You type in: three fractions and the count of one sub-group.
# It prints: the total number of slots in the warehouse.

def total_slots(frac_group1, frac_group2, frac_of_remainder, known_count):
    # Add up the two main fractions to find what share is already assigned.
    assigned = frac_group1 + frac_group2

    # What is left over after the two main groups.
    remainder_frac = 1 - assigned

    # The third group is a fraction of that remainder.
    third_group_frac = remainder_frac * frac_of_remainder

    # We know how many slots the third group has, so divide to find the total.
    total = known_count / third_group_frac

    return total

print("Let's find the total number of warehouse slots.")

# Ask for the fraction going to the first group (type as a decimal, e.g. 0.333 for 1/3).
frac_group1 = float(input("Fraction of total for group 1 (e.g. 0.3333 for 1/3): "))

# Ask for the fraction going to the second group.
frac_group2 = float(input("Fraction of total for group 2 (e.g. 0.1667 for 1/6): "))

# Ask for the fraction of the remainder going to the third group.
frac_of_remainder = float(input("Fraction of the remainder for group 3 (e.g. 0.3333 for 1/3): "))

# Ask for the known slot count of the third group.
known_count = int(input("Known count for group 3: "))

# Do the math.
result = total_slots(frac_group1, frac_group2, frac_of_remainder, known_count)

# Show as a whole number if the result is exact.
result = int(result) if result == int(result) else round(result, 2)

print("Total warehouse slots:", result)

# Warehouse Slot Calculator

This skill finds the total number of slots in a warehouse when three groups each receive a fractional share and only the count of the third group is known. It reconstructs the total by reversing the chain of fractions. It takes four inputs: two main fractions, one fraction of the remainder, and the known count of the third group. It prints the total slot count.

---

## Dependencies

No libraries required. Runs on standard Python.

---

## Inputs

- **Group 1 fraction**: The share of the total going to the first slot group. Enter as a decimal (e.g. 0.3333 for one third) in Option 1, or as a numerator and denominator pair in Option 2.
- **Group 2 fraction**: The share of the total going to the second slot group. Same format as Group 1.
- **Group 3 fraction of the remainder**: The share of what remains after Groups 1 and 2 that goes to the third group. Same format.
- **Known count for Group 3**: The exact number of slots in Group 3. A whole number.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Greet the user

Print a short sentence telling the user what the program will figure out.

### Step 2 — Collect Group 1 fraction

Ask the user to type in the fraction of the total assigned to Group 1.

In Option 1, accept a decimal. In Option 2, ask for the numerator first, then the denominator, and combine them into an exact fraction.

### Step 3 — Collect Group 2 fraction

Ask the same way for the fraction of the total assigned to Group 2.

### Step 4 — Collect the Group 3 fraction of the remainder

Ask the user for the fraction of what remains after Groups 1 and 2 that goes to Group 3. Use the same format as above.

### Step 5 — Collect the known count for Group 3

Ask the user to type in the exact number of slots in Group 3. This must be a whole number.

### Step 6 — Calculate the total

Add the Group 1 and Group 2 fractions together to find how much of the total is already assigned.

Subtract that combined share from 1 to find what fraction of the total remains.

Multiply the remainder fraction by the Group 3 fraction to find what share of the total Group 3 represents.

Divide the known Group 3 count by that share to recover the total.

### Step 7 — Print the result

If the result is a whole number, print it as a whole number. If not, round it to two decimal places.

Print the result with a short label that names the scenario: "Total warehouse slots: [result]".
