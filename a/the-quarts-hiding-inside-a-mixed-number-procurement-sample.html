THE QUARTS HIDING INSIDE A MIXED-NUMBER PROCUREMENT SAMPLE

In this article ❧ Procurement teams routinely receive quantities in mixed units — some suppliers report in gallons, others in quarts. Without a reliable conversion step, combined totals are meaningless and purchase orders risk being underfilled or over-ordered. This problem illustrates how unit conversion with mixed numbers underpins everyday supply-chain arithmetic, and how a Python script and a reusable Claude skill can make that calculation repeatable and error-free.

The Problem

A procurement analyst at a beverage company is comparing two distribution centers' sample requests for a new industrial sanitizer. Distribution Center A requested a 3¾-gallon sample. Distribution Center B requested 7 quarts more than Distribution Center A. Each gallon equals 4 quarts. How many quarts of sanitizer did both distribution centers request in total?

  • a) 37 quarts
  • b) 22/4
  • c) 14/4
  • d) 4/37

Why This Math Matters

When procurement teams compare sample requests across distribution centers, quantities often arrive in mixed units — some in gallons, others in quarts. Without converting to a common unit first, totals are meaningless and orders can be under- or over-filled. This problem also tests critical reading: the phrase "7 quarts more than Distribution Center A" is easy to misread as simply "7 quarts." A reader who misses the word "more" skips the addition step entirely and reaches a wrong total — a costly error in a real purchase order.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

  1. Convert Center A's sample to quarts: 3¾ × 4 = 15/4 × 4 = 15 quarts
  2. Calculate Center B's sample: 15 + 7 = 22 quarts
  3. Calculate total quarts: 15 + 22 = 37 quarts
  4. Conclusion: Both distribution centers requested a combined total of 37 quarts. Correct answer: a) ✅

Mathematical Background

This problem applies unit conversion with mixed numbers — converting a quantity expressed in one unit (gallons) into a smaller unit (quarts) using a fixed ratio (1:4), then performing addition. Mixed numbers like 3¾ must first be converted to improper fractions (15/4) before multiplying cleanly. An improper fraction has a numerator equal to or larger than its denominator: 15/4 means "15 parts where each whole contains 4 parts." It is the computation-ready form of a mixed number — you read in mixed numbers, you calculate in improper fractions.

Medieval Islamic scholars, particularly those working in the tradition of al-Khwārizmī, systematized fraction arithmetic precisely because commercial and measurement contexts constantly required moving between unit scales. Al-Khwārizmī's Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr waʾl-muqābala (c. 820 CE) included practical problems involving fractional land measurements in qirats — used to divide inherited property under Islamic inheritance law — where scribes needed to convert everything to a common unit before summing, exactly the operation above.

Python Version

The script below accepts five inputs — Center A's whole gallons, its fractional part as a numerator and denominator, the quarts-per-gallon conversion rate, and Center B's extra quarts — and returns the combined total. It uses Python's built-in fractions.Fraction class to keep the arithmetic exact, avoiding floating-point rounding on non-terminating decimals.

from fractions import Fraction

def total_sample_quarts(whole, frac_num, frac_den, conversion_rate, extra_quarts):
    center_a_gallons = Fraction(whole) + Fraction(frac_num, frac_den)
    center_a_quarts  = center_a_gallons * conversion_rate
    center_b_quarts  = center_a_quarts + extra_quarts
    total = center_a_quarts + center_b_quarts
    return int(total) if total == int(total) else float(total)

whole    = int(input("Distribution Center A sample — whole gallons (e.g. 3): "))
frac_num = int(input("Fractional part — numerator (e.g. 3): "))
frac_den = int(input("Fractional part — denominator (e.g. 4): "))
rate     = int(input("Quarts per gallon (e.g. 4): "))
extra    = int(input("Extra quarts Center B ordered above Center A (e.g. 7): "))

result = total_sample_quarts(whole, frac_num, frac_den, rate, extra)
print(f"\nCombined sample request — both distribution centers: {result} quarts")

Paste the code into Windows Terminal, run python, and enter each value when prompted. The script returns the combined quart count for any valid pair of sample sizes.

# Sanitizer Sample Quarts Solver

This skill takes a mixed-number gallon quantity from Distribution Center A and a fixed quart
surplus from Distribution Center B, converts everything to a common unit (quarts), and returns
the combined total quarts requested by both centers. It handles the fractional part of the gallon
amount as a separate numerator and denominator so the conversion remains exact.

---

## Dependencies

No external libraries required. This skill uses only basic arithmetic and, optionally, exact
fraction arithmetic available in Python's standard library.

---

## Inputs

- **Whole gallons (Center A):** The whole-number part of Center A's gallon quantity. Must be a non-negative integer (e.g., 3).
- **Fraction numerator (Center A):** The top number of the fractional gallon part (e.g., 3 for ¾).
- **Fraction denominator (Center A):** The bottom number of the fractional gallon part (e.g., 4 for ¾). Must not be zero.
- **Quarts per gallon:** The unit conversion rate. Standard value is 4, but the skill accepts any positive integer.
- **Extra quarts (Center B):** The number of additional quarts Center B requested above Center A's quart equivalent. Must be a non-negative integer.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Collect Inputs

Prompt the user to supply each of the five inputs listed above, one at a time. Display a short,
friendly label for each prompt that names the distribution center and the unit (e.g.,
"Distribution Center A sample — whole gallons"). Accept only valid numbers; if a
non-numeric value is entered, ask again.

### Step 2 — Reconstruct Center A's Gallon Quantity

Combine the whole gallon part and the fractional part into a single quantity by dividing the
numerator by the denominator and adding the result to the whole number. This represents the
exact gallon volume Center A requested.

### Step 3 — Convert Center A's Gallons to Quarts

Multiply Center A's full gallon quantity by the quarts-per-gallon conversion rate. The result
is Center A's total request expressed in quarts.

### Step 4 — Compute Center B's Quart Quantity

Add the extra quarts to Center A's quart equivalent. The result is Center B's total quart request.

### Step 5 — Compute the Combined Total

Add Center A's quarts and Center B's quarts together. If the result is a whole number,
display it without a decimal point. Otherwise, display the decimal value.

### Step 6 — Display the Result

Print a single output line in this format:

Combined sample request — both distribution centers: [result] quarts

The message must name both distribution centers and state the unit clearly.
