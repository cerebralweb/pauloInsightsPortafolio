THE LEFTOVER SHARE EVERY BUDGET DECIDES BEFORE YOU DO

In this article ❧ Budget lines are not all chosen. Once a team assigns shares to the items it controls, the final line absorbs whatever remains, and its size is fixed by arithmetic rather than judgment. This problem shows how adding fractions and subtracting from one whole determines a residual line, and how Python and a Claude Skill turn that logic into a reusable tool.

The Problem

An operations team receives a quarterly budget. They allocate 2/5 of the budget to software licenses. They allocate 3/8 of the budget to cloud hosting. They use the rest to buy support contracts.

What fraction of the budget goes to support contracts?

  • a) 22.5% of his budget
  • b) 3/12 of his budget
  • c) 3/20 of his budget
  • d) 17/40 of his budget
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: a) 22.5% of the budget ✅

Math Solution

Both spent fractions go over a shared bottom of 40, so 2/5 becomes 16/40 and 3/8 becomes 15/40. Add them to get 31/40 spent, then subtract from one whole to leave 9/40, which converts to 22.5%.

Lock 1/40 as 2.5%, then multiply by the numerator. 9 times 2.5% gives 22.5%.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Shared bottom: 2/5 = 16/40 and 3/8 = 15/40
  2. Spent total: 16/40 + 15/40 = 31/40
  3. Leftover: 1 − 31/40 = 9/40
  4. As a percent: 9/40 = 22.5%

The percent step can be read in plain words. Per cent means per hundred. Forty goes into one hundred two and a half times, so multiply nine by two and a half.

Natural Language Conversion

The following sequence reaches 22.5 per cent by reading the numbers aloud:

  1. Start: nine fortieths, written 9/40
  2. Double both parts: 9/40 = 18/80
  3. Two nines: two times nine is eighteen
  4. Half of nine: half of 9/40 adds four and a half
  5. Read it off: eighteen and four and a half make 22.5 per cent

Historical Background

Splitting a whole into named parts and tracking the remainder is one of the oldest tasks in recorded arithmetic. Egyptian scribes worked this way in the Rhind papyrus around 1550 BCE, writing most fractions as sums of unit parts with a numerator of one. The same residual logic later anchored merchant ledgers and partnership accounts in medieval trade, where one party's share was whatever the others did not claim.

Python Version

This script adds two spent fractions, subtracts them from one whole, and reports the leftover for any pair of allocations.

# What this does: finds the budget fraction left for the last line item, then shows it as a percent.
# Where it came from: an operations team spent two fractions of a budget and the rest went to support contracts.
# The math idea: add two fractions, then subtract from one whole, then turn the leftover into a percent.
# You type in: the top and bottom of each of the two fractions that were spent.
# It prints: the leftover fraction and the matching percent.

# This function does the math.
def leftover_share(top1, bottom1, top2, bottom2):
    # Find a shared bottom number for both fractions.
    shared_bottom = bottom1 * bottom2
    # Rebuild each fraction over that shared bottom.
    part1 = top1 * bottom2
    part2 = top2 * bottom1
    # Add the two spent parts together.
    spent_top = part1 + part2
    # The leftover top is the whole minus what was spent.
    leftover_top = shared_bottom - spent_top
    # Turn the leftover fraction into a percent.
    percent = leftover_top / shared_bottom * 100
    return leftover_top, shared_bottom, percent

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find the budget share left for support contracts.")

# Ask for the first spent fraction.
top1 = float(input("First allocation, top number: "))
bottom1 = float(input("First allocation, bottom number: "))

# Ask for the second spent fraction.
top2 = float(input("Second allocation, top number: "))
bottom2 = float(input("Second allocation, bottom number: "))

# Do the math.
leftover_top, shared_bottom, percent = leftover_share(top1, bottom1, top2, bottom2)

# Show whole numbers cleanly when possible.
leftover_top = int(leftover_top) if leftover_top.is_integer() else leftover_top
shared_bottom = int(shared_bottom) if shared_bottom.is_integer() else shared_bottom
percent = int(percent) if percent.is_integer() else round(percent, 4)

# Print the answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Support contracts share:", str(leftover_top) + "/" + str(shared_bottom), "which is", str(percent) + "%")

Paste it into a Python prompt, enter the four numbers, and it returns the leftover as a fraction with its percent. For 2, 5, 3, 8 it returns 9/40 and 22.5%.

# Budget Leftover Share

This skill finds the share of a budget that remains for a final line item after two earlier fractions have been spent. It takes two spent fractions, each as a top and bottom number, adds them, subtracts the sum from one whole, and reports the leftover both as a reduced fraction and as a percent. The output is a short business answer naming the remaining share.

---

## Dependencies

No third party libraries. Two paths are available.

- Plain arithmetic, no tools required.
- The standard `fractions` tool, used only to reduce the leftover fraction to lowest terms automatically.

---

## Inputs

- **First spent fraction**: a top number and a bottom number for the first allocation. Bottom number cannot be zero.
- **Second spent fraction**: a top number and a bottom number for the second allocation. Bottom number cannot be zero.

The two fractions together should not exceed one whole. If they do, the leftover comes out negative, which signals overspending.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Collect the two spent fractions

Ask for the top and bottom number of the first allocation. Then ask for the top and bottom number of the second allocation. Confirm that neither bottom number is zero before continuing.

### Step 2 — Put both fractions over a shared bottom

Find a common bottom number for the two fractions. Rebuild each fraction over that shared bottom so they can be added. If you use the fractions tool instead, skip the manual rebuild and let the tool keep the values exact.

### Step 3 — Add the spent shares

Add the two rebuilt fractions to get the total spent share as a single fraction over the shared bottom.

### Step 4 — Subtract from one whole

Subtract the total spent share from one whole. The result is the leftover share for the final line item. If the result is below zero, report that the two allocations already exceed the budget and stop.

### Step 5 — Reduce and convert

Reduce the leftover fraction to lowest terms. Then convert it to a percent by dividing the top by the bottom and multiplying by 100. Show the percent as a whole number when it lands clean, otherwise round to a few decimal places.

### Step 6 — Report the result

State the leftover as a fraction and as a percent in one short business sentence, naming the final line item. Example wording: support contracts share is 9/40, which is 22.5%.

---

## Example Trigger Prompts

- "I spent 2/5 of the budget on licenses and 3/8 on hosting. What is left for support?"
- "Two thirds went to payroll and one fifth to rent. What fraction remains?"
- "Turn the leftover budget share into a percent for me."
- "Find the remaining slice of the budget after these two allocations."

---

## Quality Check Before Delivering

- [ ] Both bottom numbers were checked against zero.
- [ ] The two spent fractions were added over a shared bottom.
- [ ] The leftover came from subtracting the spent total from one whole.
- [ ] A negative leftover is caught and reported as overspending.
- [ ] The leftover is shown reduced and as a percent.
- [ ] The final sentence names the line item in plain business terms.
