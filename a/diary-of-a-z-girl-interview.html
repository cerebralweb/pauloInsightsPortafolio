Q&A SESSION ABOUT THE DIARY OF A Z GIRL

In this article ❧ I created this interview to explain what is Diary of Z Girl. Hope you enjoy it!

TV: In a nutshell, what is Diary of a Z Girl about? CL: It's a contemporary romance in the era of artificial intelligence.

TV: You have said that Diary of a Z Girl is different. How so? CL: It is a romance but is not predictable because it plays with other genres, like fantasy, sci-fi, and classic literature. It is also an ethnography of New York City.

TV: In the conversation with Ann Gibson, you talked about new trends in the book market, like genre hybridization. Why do you think that is? CL: Readers and writers are always pushing the logic and limits of genres. Stagnation is not an option in any market. Diary of a Z Girl fits into the trend. It's a romance, but it interacts with fantasy and science fiction.

TV: Do you think the health of the book ecosystem is good today? CL: Absolutely. Authors have tools like self-publishing to escape bureaucracy. The Internet has plenty of open-source tools to help us. However, the main ingredient in the ecosystem is the presence of bookstagramers and the booktok phenomenon.

TV: Is there classic literature in your novel? Isn't that "boring"? CL: What makes some books boring is their focus on intellectual vanity. People take those books and sell them at the used books store. But literature is the fuel for any piece of fiction. You can observe it working well, for example, in the work of Ali Hazelwood. Where do you think comedy comes from? It comes from Antique Theater and the Medieval Literature. My novel retells three fairy tales: The Princess and the Frog. Orpheus and Eurydice, and The Minotaur and Ariadne.

TV: How do you manage to mix three ancient tales in a romance novel? CL: Many authors do it. In the end, all the stories have been told. Art is not about creating totally new things but re-creating. My book is basically a retelling of The Minotaur and Ariadne. Orpheus and Eurydice helped me build some scenes. The Princess and the Frog has a special meaning for me. When I read it, it helped me to redo several parts of the plot. I retell the story in a whole chapter, mixed with the elements of science fiction and fantasy I incorporated.

TV: If you consider yourself a romance writer, why do you give so much importance to fantasy? CL: I guess it has to do with the enormous influence of Gabriel García Márquez and his magic realism. This is one of the things that makes this book unique: the fact that I'm merging very different traditions.

TV: Is your novel "spicy"? CL: [laughs] It is spicy. It's not the kind of spicy that leaves nothing to the imagination. I can tell you that my main characters explore seduction and sex constantly. The question is not surprising. The demand for spicy content in novels has soared, and, again, one of the reasons is Instagram, where women feel free to express their penchants for "smutty" content. Also, this is a historical phenomenon. Not long ago, American women found it difficult to get rid of sexual tabus, and the romance novel was an accepted way to elaborate on their sexual feelings. What is interesting is that in the present day, the spicy agenda continues to be a women's thing.
