ONE-FIFTH OF WHAT? THE FRACTION TRAP IN BILLABLE HOUR ALLOCATION

In this article ❧ Resource allocation decisions hide some of the most consequential arithmetic in business. When a fixed capacity — time, budget, headcount — is divided by a fraction, the size of each portion and the gap between them determines how projects are staffed, scoped, and delivered. This article shows how a single fractional split becomes a project management calculation, a Python automation, and a reusable skill — and why reading the unit label is the most important step in all three.

The Problem

A consulting team has 16 billable hours available for a client engagement. The project manager allocates 2/5 of those hours to the discovery phase and the remaining hours to the delivery phase.

What is the difference in hours between the time allocated to the delivery phase and the time allocated to the discovery phase?

  • a) 9 and 1/5
  • b) 1/5
  • c) 3 and 1/5
  • d) 9.25
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: c) 3 and 1/5 ✅

Math Structure Preserved

This problem applies fraction of a whole — multiplying a total by a given fraction — and then subtraction of complementary parts to find the difference. The two fractions (2/5 and 3/5) sum to 1, so they partition the 16 hours without remainder; the difference equals (3/5 − 2/5) × 16 = 1/5 × 16 = 3.2 hours.

Key insight: subtracting the fractions before multiplying (1/5 × 16) is equivalent to multiplying each fraction separately and then subtracting — the distributive property makes both paths identical.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Shortcut method — subtract the fractions first, then multiply:

  1. Difference in fractions: 3/5 − 2/5 = 1/5
  2. Apply to total: 1/5 × 16 = 16/5 = 3.2 hours → 3 and 1/5

Long method — multiply each fraction separately, then subtract:

  1. Discovery phase allocation: 2/5 × 16 = 32/5 = 6.4 hours
  2. Delivery phase allocation: 3/5 × 16 = 48/5 = 9.6 hours
  3. Difference: 9.6 − 6.4 = 3.2 hours → 3 and 1/5

Both methods yield the same result. The shortcut works because of the distributive property: 3/5 × 16 − 2/5 × 16 = (3/5 − 2/5) × 16.

Conclusion: The delivery phase receives 3 and 1/5 more hours than the discovery phase — answer c).

⚠️ Two Pitfalls in the Answer Choices

Pitfall 1 — Answer b) "1/5": The fraction 1/5 appears in the shortcut and can feel like a complete answer, but it is not. 1/5 is only the fractional difference; the actual difference is 1/5 of 16 hours = 3.2 hours. Notice also that 1/5 of a pound (3.2 oz) and 1/5 of an ounce (0.2 oz) are two entirely different numbers — one is 16× larger than the other. Answer b) exploits exactly this confusion: it gives you the fraction and hopes you forget to ask, one-fifth of what?

Pitfall 2 — Answer c) "3 and 1/5": The result 3.2 hours would not normally be written as "3 and 1/5" in everyday or business contexts — you would say 3.2. Yet c) is still the best available answer, and on a test that means it is correct. Do not let unconventional phrasing push you toward a numerically wrong choice.

📝 Test-Taking Lesson: The Words Tell You the Unit

When working through a problem quickly, it is tempting to focus only on the numbers and ignore the words. But the words tell you what unit you are dealing with. Whenever you see a fraction like 1/5, pause and ask: one-fifth of what? Is it 1/5 lb, 1/5 oz, 1/5 of the total? That one-second check — "one-fifth of what?" — is what separates the right answer from a trap. All of the following are equivalent and valid:

  • 1/5 of 16 ounces
  • (1/5) × 16 oz
  • 3.2 oz
  • 3 and 1/5 oz

Why This Math Matters for the Business Problem

Resource allocation is one of the most common decisions in project management and consulting. When a fixed capacity — billable hours, budget, headcount — must be split between phases or workstreams, knowing the exact size of each portion and the gap between them is essential. The difference between the delivery and discovery allocations tells the project manager how much more execution capacity is committed relative to research, which directly informs staffing, scheduling, and client expectations. Getting this wrong by even a fraction can cause scope creep or under-resourcing.

Mathematical Background

This problem applies fraction of a whole — finding a part of a quantity by multiplying the total by a given fraction — combined with the distributive property, which allows you to factor out a common multiplier and subtract the fractions before multiplying, saving a step.

The history of fractions spans several civilizations. The earliest known systematic use appears in ancient Egypt, where scribes recorded calculations almost exclusively using unit fractions (fractions with numerator 1, such as 1/2, 1/3, 1/5) in documents like the Rhind Mathematical Papyrus, dating to around 1650 BCE. Expressing 2/5 as an Egyptian scribe would have required decomposing it into a sum of distinct unit fractions — a non-trivial exercise. Ancient Babylonian mathematics used a base-60 (sexagesimal) positional system that allowed fractional quantities to be expressed with great precision, and traces of it survive today in how we measure time and angles. Greek mathematicians, particularly in the tradition of Euclid, treated ratios and proportions geometrically rather than as standalone numbers. It was medieval Islamic scholars — notably al-Khwārizmī and al-Uqlidisi — who advanced the notation and arithmetic of fractions closer to the form we use today, work that was later transmitted to Europe through Latin translations in the 12th century.

Python Version

The following script generalizes the problem: enter any total, any fraction numerator, and any denominator, and it returns both allocations and the difference between them. It mirrors the same arithmetic used in the step-by-step breakdown above and runs directly in Windows Terminal.

Option 1 — Pure Python (no imports required):

def allocation_difference(total_hours, numerator, denominator):
    phase_1 = (numerator / denominator) * total_hours
    phase_2 = ((denominator - numerator) / denominator) * total_hours
    difference = phase_2 - phase_1
    return phase_1, phase_2, difference

print("--- Consulting Project: Billable Hours Allocation ---")
total_hours = float(input("Total billable hours available: "))
numerator   = float(input("Numerator of the first-phase fraction (e.g. 2 for 2/5): "))
denominator = float(input("Denominator of the fraction (e.g. 5 for 2/5): "))

phase_1, phase_2, difference = allocation_difference(total_hours, numerator, denominator)

def fmt(n):
    return int(n) if n == int(n) else round(n, 4)

print(f"\nDiscovery phase allocation : {fmt(phase_1)} hours")
print(f"Delivery phase allocation  : {fmt(phase_2)} hours")
print(f"Difference (delivery - discovery): {fmt(difference)} hours")

Option 2 — Exact Fractions using Python's built-in fractions.Fraction module. When the denominator does not divide evenly into the total, floating-point arithmetic can introduce small rounding errors; this variant eliminates them entirely and displays each result as both an exact fraction and a decimal approximation side by side:

from fractions import Fraction

def allocation_difference(total_hours, numerator, denominator):
    total  = Fraction(total_hours).limit_denominator(1000)
    f1     = Fraction(numerator, denominator)
    f2     = 1 - f1
    phase_1    = f1 * total
    phase_2    = f2 * total
    difference = phase_2 - phase_1
    return phase_1, phase_2, difference

print("--- Consulting Project: Billable Hours Allocation (Exact Fractions) ---")
total_hours = int(input("Total billable hours available (whole number): "))
numerator   = int(input("Numerator of the first-phase fraction (e.g. 2 for 2/5): "))
denominator = int(input("Denominator of the fraction (e.g. 5 for 2/5): "))

p1, p2, diff = allocation_difference(total_hours, numerator, denominator)

print(f"\nDiscovery phase allocation : {p1} hrs  ({float(p1):.4g} hrs)")
print(f"Delivery phase allocation  : {p2} hrs  ({float(p2):.4g} hrs)")
print(f"Difference (delivery - discovery): {diff} hrs  ({float(diff):.4g} hrs)")

To run either option: open Windows Terminal, type python, paste the code, press Enter, then enter the three requested values. For the original problem (16 hours, 2/5 split) both scripts return 3.2 hours → 3 and 1/5.

The Skill File

The Python logic above was packaged as a reusable Claude skill. The Markdown document below expresses every step in plain English — no code — so it can be executed by following instructions, not by running the original script.

---
name: billable-hours-allocation-calculator
description: Calculates the allocation of a fixed resource (such as billable hours or budget) between
two complementary phases using a given fraction, then reports each phase's share and the difference
between them. Trigger this skill whenever the user wants to split a total quantity into two fractional
portions and find the gap between them, even if they don't use mathematical language — for example
"how much more time goes to delivery than discovery?" or "split my hours between two phases."
---

# Billable Hours Allocation Calculator

This skill takes a total quantity of a resource — such as billable hours, a budget, or inventory units
— and a fraction that defines how much goes to the first phase. It computes both phase allocations using
that fraction and its complement, then calculates the difference between the two. It also explains the
arithmetic in two equivalent ways: a shortcut (subtract fractions first, then multiply) and a long method
(multiply each fraction separately, then subtract). Results are displayed with friendly business-context labels.

---

## Dependencies

List any libraries or tools Claude needs to have available:
- **Pure arithmetic** — uses only basic Python operations; no imports needed.
- **Exact-fraction variant** — uses Python's built-in `fractions.Fraction` module (part of the standard library) to eliminate floating-point rounding. Recommended when the fraction does not divide evenly into a decimal.

---

## Inputs

- **Total resource quantity**: A number representing the full amount to be allocated (e.g., 16 billable hours). Accepts whole numbers or decimals.
- **Fraction numerator**: The top number of the fraction assigned to the first phase (e.g., 2 for a 2/5 split).
- **Fraction denominator**: The bottom number shared by both phases (e.g., 5 for a 2/5 split). Must be a positive integer greater than the numerator.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Greet and orient the user

Print a short header that names the business scenario so the user knows what the program is calculating. Example: `--- Consulting Project: Billable Hours Allocation ---`

### Step 2 — Collect inputs interactively

Prompt the user to enter each of the three required values one at a time:
1. Total billable hours available
2. The numerator of the first-phase fraction
3. The denominator of the fraction

Accept decimal input for total hours. Accept whole-number input for the numerator and denominator. Do not use command-line arguments.

### Step 3 — Compute phase allocations

Calculate the first-phase share by multiplying the total by the given fraction (numerator ÷ denominator). Calculate the second-phase share by multiplying the total by the complementary fraction ((denominator − numerator) ÷ denominator). These two shares must sum exactly to the total.

### Step 4 — Compute the difference

Subtract the first-phase allocation from the second-phase allocation to find the gap. If the result is a whole number, display it without a decimal point. Otherwise, round to four decimal places for display (or present it as an exact fraction if using the exact-fraction variant).

### Step 5 — Display results with business-context labels

Print three clearly labelled output lines:
- Discovery phase allocation: [value] hours
- Delivery phase allocation: [value] hours
- Difference (delivery − discovery): [value] hours

Labels must reflect the business scenario, not generic variable names.

---

## Exact-Fraction Variant (Optional)

When the denominator does not divide evenly into the total, floating-point arithmetic may introduce small rounding errors. In that case, use the exact-fraction variant:

- Convert all inputs to exact `Fraction` objects before performing any arithmetic.
- Carry out all four operations (two multiplications, one subtraction for the complement, one subtraction for the difference) using exact fractions.
- Display each result as both an exact fraction and a decimal approximation side by side, so the user sees both representations.
