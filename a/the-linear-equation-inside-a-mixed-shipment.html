1,888 SAMPLE BAGS: THE LINEAR EQUATION INSIDE A MIXED SHIPMENT

In this article ❧ Operational teams that manage ingredient sourcing rarely deal with one shipment at a time. When two deliveries arrive together and only the total weight and the gap between them are documented, recovering each individual quantity requires algebraic thinking — not just arithmetic. This article shows how substitution in a linear equation unlocks the missing weight, and how a unit-conversion step turns that weight into an exact production count.

The Problem

A food manufacturing company receives two ingredient shipments. The shipment of dried herbs weighs 17 pounds more than the shipment of spice blend. Together, both shipments weigh 253 pounds.

If the spice blend is to be repackaged into sample bags that each hold exactly one ounce, how many sample bags will be needed? (One pound equals 16 ounces.)

  • a) 1,888
  • b) 1.8
  • c) 188
  • d) 118
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1,888 ✅

Math Solution

Because the two shipments together weigh a known total and differ by a known amount, the problem reduces to a single linear equation: 2p + 17 = 253. Solving for p gives the spice blend weight in pounds, which is then multiplied by 16 to convert to ounces — one ounce per sample bag, giving 1,888 bags.

The unit-conversion step is the operational bridge: skipping it or inverting it would produce a bag count that is off by a factor of 16 — a costly production error.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final count is derived:

  1. Define the unknown: let p = weight of the spice blend shipment in pounds
  2. Express the herbs shipment: dried herbs = p + 17 lb
  3. Write the total-weight equation: p + (p + 17) = 253
  4. Combine like terms: 2p + 17 = 253
  5. Isolate the variable: 2p = 253 − 17 = 236 → p = 236 ÷ 2 = 118 lb
  6. Convert to ounces: 118 lb × 16 oz/lb = 1,888 oz
  7. Count sample bags: 1,888 oz ÷ 1 oz per bag = 1,888 bags
  8. Conclusion: The spice blend shipment weighs 118 pounds, which fills exactly 1,888 one-ounce sample bags.

Historical Background

The Arabic word al-jabr, from which the English word algebra derives, first appeared in the title of a 9th-century treatise by the Baghdad scholar Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī: Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wal-muqābala ("The Concise Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing," c. 820 CE). Al-Khwārizmī described al-jabr as the operation of moving a subtracted term to the other side of an equation — exactly what happens when 17 is subtracted from both sides to isolate 2p. His companion operation, al-muqābala, meant combining like terms — exactly what produces 2p + 17 from p + (p + 17).

Even older roots exist. The 3rd-century Alexandrian mathematician Diophantus, in his Arithmetica, solved problems involving multiple unknowns by expressing one quantity in terms of another and substituting — the same substitution technique at work here. Though Diophantus worked with specific numerical problems rather than general symbolic rules, historians of mathematics regard him as a forerunner of algebraic thinking in the Western tradition. Both traditions converge in this problem: set up two conditions, substitute one into the other, and solve.

Python Version

The script below generalizes the calculation: it accepts any combined shipment weight, weight difference, and bag capacity, making it reusable across different ingredient pairs and packaging formats.

def calculate_sample_bags(total_weight_lb, weight_diff_lb, bag_capacity_oz):
    oz_per_lb = 16
    lighter_shipment_lb = (total_weight_lb - weight_diff_lb) / 2
    lighter_shipment_oz = lighter_shipment_lb * oz_per_lb
    bags = lighter_shipment_oz / bag_capacity_oz
    return int(bags) if bags == int(bags) else bags

print("Sample bag calculator — mixed ingredient shipment")
total_weight = float(input("Total combined shipment weight (lb): "))
weight_diff  = float(input("Weight difference between the two shipments (lb): "))
bag_capacity = float(input("Capacity per sample bag (oz): "))

result = calculate_sample_bags(total_weight, weight_diff, bag_capacity)
print(f"Sample bags needed from the lighter shipment: {result}")

Paste the code into a Python session in Windows Terminal, enter each value when prompted, and the program returns the exact bag count — for this problem's inputs, 1888.
