60 HOURS, 24 ATTENDEES, AND ONE DIVISION RULE THAT SETTLES IT

In this article ❧ Teams routinely commit a fixed pool of time, budget, or seats before they know who will take part. When some members drop out, the share that each remaining person receives shifts, and a short arithmetic step keeps the plan accurate. This problem shows how a small chain of operations can model who gets what when a group shrinks, and how Python turns that logic into a tool you can reuse for any team and any pool.

The Problem

A learning lead at a software firm booked 5 blocks of 12 hours of workshop time, 60 hours in total, to share with the team during an onboarding week. The team has 27 members. On the workshop day, 1/9 of the members were out.

If the lead splits the 60 hours equally among the members who showed up, how many hours does each one receive?

  • a) 2 and 1/2
  • b) 1
  • c) 2
  • d) 5 and 1/3
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: a) 2 and 1/2 ✅

Math Solution

First multiply the blocks to get the total, then find the absent count and subtract it from the team. Divide the total hours by the members present to reach the share per person.

The absent share is removed before the division, so the same total is split across fewer people and each share rises.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Total hours: 5 × 12 = 60
  2. Absent count: 27 × 1/9 = 3
  3. Present count: 27 − 3 = 24
  4. Share per person: 60 ÷ 24 = 2 and 1/2

Historical Background

Splitting a shared pool among the people who take part is one of the oldest tasks in commercial arithmetic. Medieval merchants and guilds divided costs, profits, and goods among active partners, and Leonardo of Pisa, known as Fibonacci, spread these methods across Europe in his 1202 Liber Abaci, which taught traders how to share quantities fairly. The same reasoning still governs how teams allocate hours, seats, and budgets once the final headcount is known.

Python Version

The script builds the total, removes the absent share, and divides the rest among the people present, so it works for any team and any pool.

# What this does. Works out how many workshop hours each present member gets.
# Where it came from. A learning lead booked blocks of hours and split them among the people who showed up.
# The math idea. Find the total, take out the absent share, then divide the rest.
# You type in. The number of blocks, the hours per block, the team size, and the absent share as two numbers.
# It prints. How many hours each present member receives.

# This function does the math.
def hours_per_member(blocks, hours_each_block, team_size, absent_top, absent_bottom):
    # Build the total hours from the blocks.
    total_hours = blocks * hours_each_block
    # Work out how many people were absent.
    absent = team_size * absent_top / absent_bottom
    # The rest showed up.
    present = team_size - absent
    # Split the total hours among the people present.
    return total_hours / present

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find how many hours each person gets.")

# Ask for the number of blocks.
blocks = int(input("Number of blocks "))

# Ask for the hours in one block.
hours_each_block = int(input("Hours per block "))

# Ask for the team size.
team_size = int(input("Team size "))

# Ask for the top of the absent share. For one ninth this is 1.
absent_top = int(input("Absent share top number "))

# Ask for the bottom of the absent share. For one ninth this is 9.
absent_bottom = int(input("Absent share bottom number "))

# Do the math.
result = hours_per_member(blocks, hours_each_block, team_size, absent_top, absent_bottom)

# Show a whole number when it comes out even.
result = int(result) if result.is_integer() else result

# Print the answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Hours each present member receives", result)

# What this does. Works out the exact hours each present member gets, as a fraction.
# Where it came from. A learning lead booked blocks of hours and split them among the people who showed up.
# The math idea. Find the total, take out the absent share, then divide, keeping an exact fraction.
# You type in. The number of blocks, the hours per block, the team size, and the absent share as two numbers.
# It prints. The exact hours each present member receives, as a fraction.

# Bring in a tool that keeps exact fractions instead of decimals.
from fractions import Fraction

# This function does the math.
def hours_per_member(blocks, hours_each_block, team_size, absent_top, absent_bottom):
    # Build the total hours from the blocks.
    total_hours = blocks * hours_each_block
    # The part of the team that was absent, kept exact.
    absent_share = Fraction(absent_top, absent_bottom)
    # The people who showed up.
    present = team_size - team_size * absent_share
    # Split the total hours among the people present, kept exact.
    return Fraction(total_hours) / present

# Share Per Attendee Calculator

This skill takes a total resource built from equal blocks, removes a fractional share of the group that was absent, and divides the total equally among the people who attended. It asks for a few numbers, runs the division, and reports how much each present member receives. It can report the answer as a plain number or as an exact fraction.

---

## Dependencies

- None for the plain version.
- The Fraction class from the standard fractions library for the exact fraction version. This keeps the answer as a clean fraction such as 5/2 rather than the decimal 2.5.

---

## Inputs

- **Number of blocks** — how many equal blocks make up the total. A whole number.
- **Hours per block** — the size of one block. A whole number.
- **Team size** — the full count of members before anyone is marked absent. A whole number.
- **Absent share top number** — the top of the absent fraction. For one ninth this is 1.
- **Absent share bottom number** — the bottom of the absent fraction. For one ninth this is 9.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — State the question

Open with a short line that tells the reader what will be worked out, that each person who attended gets an equal slice of the total.

### Step 2 — Collect the inputs

Ask for each value in turn. Read the number of blocks, the hours per block, the team size, the absent share top number, and the absent share bottom number. Treat all five as whole numbers.

### Step 3 — Build the total

Multiply the number of blocks by the hours per block. This gives the full resource to be shared.

### Step 4 — Find the absent count

Take the absent share, the top number over the bottom number, and apply it to the team size. This gives how many members were away.

### Step 5 — Find the present count

Subtract the absent count from the team size. This gives the number of people who attended.

### Step 6 — Divide the total among those present

Divide the full resource by the present count. The result is the amount each attendee receives.

### Step 7 — Choose how to show the result

If a plain number is wanted, show a whole number when the result comes out even, otherwise show the decimal. If an exact answer is wanted, carry the absent share and the division as fractions throughout so the result prints as an exact fraction.

### Step 8 — Report with context

Print the result inside a short line that fits the scenario, naming what each present member receives. Never print the bare number alone.
