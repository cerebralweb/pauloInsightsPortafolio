WHEN TWO THIRDS MEETS THREE FIFTHS IN A PERFECT PAIRING

In this article ❧ Many staffing choices come down to matching two groups where every match needs one member from each side. Recruiting pairs interviewers with candidates. Mentoring pairs seniors with juniors. Onboarding pairs new hires with guides. The moment a program runs on pairs, the count drawn from one group is locked to the count drawn from the other, and fractions of each group have to land on the same whole number. This problem shows how a pairing constraint turns two fractions into a single answer, and how a small Python tool can solve the same shape of question for any two shares.

The Problem

The engineering team and the design team at a software firm run a buddy program. Each buddy pair has one engineer and one designer. Two thirds of the engineering team and three fifths of the design team will join. Every participant forms exactly one pair, so the number of engineers who join equals the number of designers who join.

What is the total headcount of the two teams, and how many people will join the program?

  • a) There are 19 people, and 12 will join
  • b) There are 30 people, and 19 will join
  • c) There are 10 people, and 7 will join
  • d) There are 10 people, and 8 will join
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer a) There are 19 people, and 12 will join ✅

Math Solution

Two thirds of engineering equals three fifths of design. Clearing the fractions (finding a common denominator) gives 10E = 9D, whose smallest whole solution is 9 engineers and 10 designers: the simplest way 10E = 9D is true is by replacing E with 3/5 and D with 2/3, which would yield 6=6. Two thirds of 9 is 6. Three fifths of 10 is 6. Note that those two fractions are not the team sizes but the smallest values that satisfy the equation.

QUESTIONCan you also start the exploration of the problem by stating (2/3)x = (3/5)y?? In the end, that is the information you start with.

Yes. That equation is the true starting point. Each pair takes one engineer and one designer, so the joiners are equal. The engineers who join are (2/3)·x. The designers who join are (3/5)·y. Setting them equal gives (2/3)·x = (3/5)·y. Everything else follows from that one line.

From there two routes open.

Clear the fractions

Multiply both sides by 15. Multiplying both sides by 15 clears the denominators and turns the equation into whole numbers You get 10x = 9y. The smallest whole solution is x = 9, y = 10.

Match the numerators

Rewrite each share with a common top number. 2/3 = 6/9 and 3/5 = 6/10. The denominators are the team sizes, 9 and 10. The top number 6 is the joiners on each side.

Both routes start from (2/3)·x = (3/5)·y and land on the same pair. The earlier substitution, x = 3/5 and y = 2/3, is the same equation read as a ratio. It gives the direction 9 to 10 without the total, which you then scale to whole people.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Set the equality. Joiners from engineering equal joiners from design, so (2/3)·E = (3/5)·D.
  2. Clear the fractions. Multiply both sides by 15, giving 10E = 9D.
  3. Find the smallest whole sizes. E must be a multiple of 9 and D a multiple of 10, so E = 9 and D = 10.
  4. Check the shares. Two thirds of 9 is 6, and three fifths of 10 is 6. Equal, as the pairing requires.
  5. Sum the headcount. 9 plus 10 is 19.
  6. Sum the joiners. 6 plus 6 is 12.

What a Unit Means Here

A unit is one equal share of a group, not a fixed count of people. Two thirds of the engineers means you cut the engineering team into 3 equal shares and 2 of them join. Three fifths of the designers means you cut the design team into 5 equal shares and 3 of them join. Each of those shares is a unit.

The trap is that an engineering share is not the same size as a design share. The two teams hold different numbers of people, so one third of one team and one fifth of the other cover different headcounts. That is why you cannot compare the shares directly yet.

Here is the move to hold onto. The same number of people join from each side, because every pair is one engineer and one designer. So the joining part of the engineering bar and the joining part of the design bar must contain the same headcount. On the engineering side that joining part is 2 units. On the design side it is 3 units. For 2 units to hold the same headcount as 3 units, the engineering unit has to be bigger than the design unit.

To count actual people you make the units the same size. You cut every unit into smaller equal pieces until an engineering piece and a design piece match. Each small piece is then one person. The engineering bar becomes 9 pieces. The design bar becomes 10 pieces. Now you can add them, because they are the same size. The yards and feet case is the same idea. 2 yards equals 6 feet because the yard is the larger unit and the foot the smaller. The Reasoning, Method by Method

Three ideas anchor the solution. First, the pairing makes the two shares equal in headcount. Second, 3 and 5 are factors of the sizes you are hunting, because a clean two thirds needs a size divisible by 3 and a clean three fifths needs a size divisible by 5. Third, the two shares must represent the same number. Those three together pin the pair 9 and 10.

The cleanest route is the common numerator. Pairing means equal joiners, so both fractions must share a top number. The smallest shared top number of 2 and 3 is 6. Rewrite both with that top number and the denominators become the team sizes.

2/3 = 6/9 and 3/5 = 6/10. Read the denominators. They are 9 and 10, the team sizes. Read the top number. It is 6, the joiners from each side. Total headcount is 19. Joiners total 12. This route assumes nothing about the total. It derives it. The bar model below shows the same move. Both teams rewrite their share to a block of 6, and those two blocks match in width even though the teams differ in size.

There is a quiet reason the total equals 19. Add the two fractions. 2/3 + 3/5 = (2·5 + 3·3)/15 = 19/15. The numerator 19 is built from 2·5 and 3·3. Those are the same two cross products that set the team sizes, since 3·3 is 9 and 5·2 is 10. So the numerator of the fraction sum is literally the headcount, 9 plus 10. It is the same arithmetic seen twice.

The pattern is real but not general. It holds only when the two top numbers share no common factor and you take the smallest solution. Swap 3/5 for 4/5. Then 2/3 + 4/5 = 22/15, yet the smallest teams are 6 and 5, a total of 11. The numerator 22 is twice the total. So treat the 19 shortcut as a structural coincidence you can explain, not a rule to trust blindly.

Assessing four ways to see it

A learner may reach the answer through several routes. Adding the fractions to get 19/15 gives the right number, but by structure, not by common sense, and only under the two conditions above. A picture that lines up 6 against 6 is a sound check, yet it starts from 9 and 10 already chosen, so it confirms rather than derives. Rescaling both wholes to fifteenths, where 10/15 of 9 is 6 and 9/15 of 10 is 6, verifies the equality at a shared scale, again on sizes already fixed. The common numerator route stands apart. It reaches the sizes with no total assumed, and it is the twin of clearing the fractions.

Is the search table a proof

Testing every pair that sums to 19 and marking each true or false is a proof by exhaustion, and the method is sound. It proves that among pairs summing to 19, exactly one passes both conditions. It does not prove the total is 19, because it assumes 19 from the start. As a proof that the answer is 19 it is circular. The deduction is stronger. It derives 9 and 10 from 10E = 9D, then adds them, so the total falls out as a result. The table below shows the exhaustion, with the single accepted pair in green.

The Array View

The array model lays out every person as a marker and draws the pairs. Nine engineers sit on the left, ten designers on the right. Six on each side join and connect one to one. The three unpaired engineers and the four unpaired designers sit to the sides, so the ribbons stay clean. The count reads 12 of 19.

The array also answers the scaffolding questions in order.

  1. An engineering unit is not the same size as a design unit. Two thirds cuts the engineering team into 3 shares, three fifths cuts the design team into 5, and shares of a 3 split and a 5 split hold different headcounts. You make them the same size by cutting both into one common piece, one person.
  2. 2 engineering units hold the same headcount as 3 design units only because the engineering unit is larger. Put numbers on it. The engineering unit holds 3 people and the design unit holds 2, so 2 times 3 is 6 and 3 times 2 is 6. This is the yards and feet case, where 2 yards equals 6 feet because the yard is the larger unit.
  3. Split the joining part of each bar into 6 equal pieces, one per joiner, then split the rest the same way. Each engineering third holds 3 people, so cut each third into 3, and the engineering bar becomes 9 pieces. Each design fifth holds 2 people, so cut each fifth into 2, and the design bar becomes 10 pieces. Together that makes 19 pieces, with 12 joining.

Historical Background

This problem holds two ideas, forcing fractions into whole units and matching two pools one to one. Both have modern histories far richer than the medieval rule of three. The groundwork is 16th century. In 1585 the Flemish engineer Simon Stevin published De Thiende, arguing that everyday calculation should use decimal fractions. Merchants and clerks adopted the idea, and splitting a quantity by proportion became routine arithmetic rather than expert craft.

Turning shares into whole counts then became a national fight in the young United States. The Constitution of 1787 required House seats to be apportioned among the states by population, yet no state can hold a fraction of a seat. Alexander Hamilton and Thomas Jefferson backed rival rounding methods, and in 1792 George Washington cast the first presidential veto to reject Hamilton's. Around 1880 the census clerk C. W. Seaton found that raising the total number of seats could cost a state a seat, a defect now called the Alabama paradox. Congress adopted the method of equal proportions in 1941, still in use today. In 1982 Michel Balinski and H. Peyton Young proved that no method can honor every share and dodge every paradox at once. The clean fit in the buddy problem is the rare case where the fractions land on whole people with nothing left over.

Matching two groups one to one grew into its own field in the 20th century. In 1955 Harold Kuhn published the Hungarian algorithm for optimal assignment, naming it for earlier work by the Hungarian mathematicians Denes Kőnig and Jenő Egerváry. In 1962 David Gale and Lloyd Shapley proved that stable pairings between two sides always exist and gave a method to build them. From the 1980s Alvin Roth turned that theory into working systems, redesigning the medical residency match, the school choice systems of New York and Boston, and kidney exchange programs that pair donors with patients. Shapley and Roth shared the 2012 Nobel Prize in economics. The buddy program is a small version of the same demand, two pools that must meet at equal whole counts.

REAL WORLD APPLICATIONS

The Hidden Number Behind Every Fair A/B Test

When a website tries two versions of a page, it splits visitors into groups. Say one test wants the crowd cut into 3 equal parts and another wants 5 equal parts. The crowd only divides with nothing left over at a size that both 3 and 5 fit into. The smallest such size is 15. That is the same move as the buddy problem, where the smallest workable team is the smallest number both denominators divide into.

Send fewer than 15 and some group ends up uneven, which quietly tilts the result. The number you pick is not a detail. It decides whether the test is fair.

The Number That Keeps Every Computer Busy

Training a model splits the data across several computers, and each computer works through its share in fixed size batches. The data shares out with nothing left over only at a size that both the number of computers and the batch size divide into. With 4 computers and batches of 6, the smallest clean size is 12.

One extra task beyond a clean size leaves a computer waiting while the others finish. The waiting computer is the same idea as the scouts who sit out. The fix is to pick a size that both counts divide.

When Work Splits Evenly, Nobody Sits Idle

A streaming system stores messages in slots called partitions and hands them to workers called consumers that read at the same time. If the partitions do not divide evenly among the consumers, some consumer gets less and sits partly idle. 6 partitions across 4 consumers splits 2, 2, 1, 1. Two consumers are underused.

Move to 8 partitions across 4 consumers and it splits 2, 2, 2, 2. Everyone carries the same load. Even sharing appears only at the right counts, which is the pairing idea in a new suit.

You Cannot Ship Two Thirds of a Server

A safe software release sends some traffic to the new version and the rest to the old one. When each server carries an equal slice of traffic, a share like two thirds works only if the fleet size lets it land on whole machines.

With 2 servers the cut falls inside a machine, and you cannot split one. With 3 servers it works, 2 new and 1 old. Three is the smallest fleet that makes two thirds exact, so the percentage you type is really a count of servers in disguise.

Python Version

This script turns the pairing logic into a reusable tool. You type the top and bottom of each team share, and it returns the smallest whole team sizes, the joiners from each side, and the totals.

# What this does: finds the smallest two team sizes so that a chosen share of each team makes equal pairs.
# Where it came from: an engineering team and a design team form one to one buddy pairs. A share of each team joins.
# The math idea: two fractions must give the same number of people, so we match their top numbers.
# You type in: the top and bottom number of each team's share, as whole numbers.
# It prints: the smallest size of each team, how many join from each side, and the totals.

# This finds the biggest number that divides two numbers evenly.
def biggest_common_factor(x, y):
    # Keep swapping until nothing is left over.
    while y:
        x, y = y, x % y
    return x

# This finds the smallest number that both numbers divide into.
def smallest_shared_multiple(x, y):
    # Multiply them, then take out the shared part.
    return x * y // biggest_common_factor(x, y)

# This works out the smallest team sizes and the people who join.
def smallest_teams(a_top, a_bottom, b_top, b_bottom):
    # Shrink the first share to its simplest form.
    g1 = biggest_common_factor(a_top, a_bottom)
    a_top, a_bottom = a_top // g1, a_bottom // g1

    # Shrink the second share to its simplest form.
    g2 = biggest_common_factor(b_top, b_bottom)
    b_top, b_bottom = b_top // g2, b_bottom // g2

    # Both sides must send the same number of people. Match the top numbers.
    joiners_each = smallest_shared_multiple(a_top, b_top)

    # The bottom numbers turn that match into a full team size.
    team_a = joiners_each // a_top * a_bottom
    team_b = joiners_each // b_top * b_bottom

    return team_a, team_b, joiners_each

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find the smallest teams that can pair off evenly.")

# Ask for the first team's share, top then bottom.
a_top = int(input("First team share, top number: "))
a_bottom = int(input("First team share, bottom number: "))

# Ask for the second team's share, top then bottom.
b_top = int(input("Second team share, top number: "))
b_bottom = int(input("Second team share, bottom number: "))

# Do the math.
team_a, team_b, joiners_each = smallest_teams(a_top, a_bottom, b_top, b_bottom)

# Add up the headcount and the people who join.
total_people = team_a + team_b
total_joiners = joiners_each * 2

# Print the answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Smallest first team size:", team_a)
print("Smallest second team size:", team_b)
print("People joining from each side:", joiners_each)
print("Total headcount:", total_people)
print("Total people in the program:", total_joiners)

Run it in a terminal, type the four numbers, and for shares of 2 over 3 and 3 over 5 it returns 9, 10, 6 each, headcount 19, and 12 in the program. The standard library offers a shorter version. Since math.gcd and math.lcm are built in, the two helper functions disappear.

# What this does: finds the smallest two team sizes so that a chosen share of each team makes equal pairs.
# Where it came from: an engineering team and a design team form one to one buddy pairs. A share of each team joins.
# The math idea: two fractions must give the same number of people, so we match their top numbers.
# You type in: the top and bottom number of each team's share, as whole numbers.
# It prints: the smallest size of each team, how many join from each side, and the totals.

# This library has ready made tools for common factors and shared multiples.
import math

# This works out the smallest team sizes and the people who join.
def smallest_teams(a_top, a_bottom, b_top, b_bottom):
    # Shrink the first share to its simplest form.
    g1 = math.gcd(a_top, a_bottom)
    a_top, a_bottom = a_top // g1, a_bottom // g1

    # Shrink the second share to its simplest form.
    g2 = math.gcd(b_top, b_bottom)
    b_top, b_bottom = b_top // g2, b_bottom // g2

    # Both sides must send the same number of people. Match the top numbers.
    joiners_each = math.lcm(a_top, b_top)

    # The bottom numbers turn that match into a full team size.
    team_a = joiners_each // a_top * a_bottom
    team_b = joiners_each // b_top * b_bottom

    return team_a, team_b, joiners_each

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find the smallest teams that can pair off evenly.")

# Ask for the first team's share, top then bottom.
a_top = int(input("First team share, top number: "))
a_bottom = int(input("First team share, bottom number: "))

# Ask for the second team's share, top then bottom.
b_top = int(input("Second team share, top number: "))
b_bottom = int(input("Second team share, bottom number: "))

# Do the math.
team_a, team_b, joiners_each = smallest_teams(a_top, a_bottom, b_top, b_bottom)

# Add up the headcount and the people who join.
total_people = team_a + team_b
total_joiners = joiners_each * 2

# Print the answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Smallest first team size:", team_a)
print("Smallest second team size:", team_b)
print("People joining from each side:", joiners_each)
print("Total headcount:", total_people)
print("Total people in the program:", total_joiners)

Both versions return the same result. The second is shorter and less error prone because it leans on tested library functions.
