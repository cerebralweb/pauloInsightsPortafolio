NIELSEN + BYZZER + QUALITATIVE: MILK SALES STUDY / WALMART

In this case study ❧ A supermarket chain investigatse underperformance in cold beverage sales. I led the integration of Byzzer and Nielsen data with qualitative tools to uncover a conversion gap.

CLIENT

Walmart

CLIENT CHALLENGES

In its flagship Mexico City store, the client—one of the country's largest supermarket chains—faced stagnant growth in cold beverage sales, particularly in the cold dairy category, despite maintaining strong inventory levels and promotional activity. Initial Nielsen analysis ruled out an overall drop in category volume. .

Byzzer data revealed a critical inefficiency: the store’s private-label dairy products, especially milk, held significant shelf space but were underperforming in sales share relative to their distribution presence. In other words, the space was there—but it wasn’t converting

    OBJECTIVES

  • Diagnose the conversion gap between shelf distribution and sales performance of private-label milk SKUs.
  • Analyze shopper behavior in relation to milk, mapping in-store journeys to understand why traffic isn't translating into proportional sales, despite promotions and stock availability.
  • Benchmark Walmart milk performance against national brands using Byzzer data.
  • Leverage Nielsen SMR’s 20 growth drivers to assess which milk-specific attributes are underperforming
  • Conduct participant observation and ethnographic mapping to assess spatial deterrents
  • Isolate the post-pandemic behavior trend of price-sensitive “mission shoppers"
  • Segment barriers to milk conversion into functional and emotional interventions.
  • Deliver integrated recommendations to sales, marketing, and trade teams.

Issues mentioned in this portfolio case

sales, packaging, conversion gap

SOLUTIONS

  • NielsenIQ + Byzzer
  • Use 12 growth-driver-attribute to research and creat hypothesis
  • Use Nielsen segmentations to understand better what was happening
  • qualitative research to test hhypothesis

    Finally, to understand non-quantifiable barriers, we conducted participant observation and in-store interviews, uncovering both functional friction and emotional resistance. Our main hhypothesis was that there were issues related to consumers journeys.

    Many shoppers couldn’t easily articulate why they bypassed the cold beverage section. Cognitive dissonance played a role: they were unaware of their habitual avoidance or gave rationalizations (e.g., “I already had milk at home”) that masked deeper behavioral patterns such as lack of brand attachment, or discomfort with cold-chain visibility.

    “Pos ya teníamos leche en la casa… y ahorita no está el dinero como pa' andar comprando de más.”

    We found that price consciousness had intensified post-COVID, especially among NSE C and D households. Shoppers were executing shorter, mission-based trips and limiting their purchases to essential categories—yet oddly, milk, though essential, wasn’t being picked up. We had to reconcile this contradiction by investigating value perception vs. functional value.

    Mission-Shoppers

    Those who avoid full-store exploration seeking just offers

    Logistical Constraints During Fieldwork

    Conducting participant observation in a busy Walmart store presented logistical challenges:

    • Gaining permission and avoiding shopper disruption
    • Tracking anonymized paths without interfering
    • Timing sessions across different days and hours to capture behavioral variety

    These realities limited the depth of individual tracking per session and required triangulation with sales and heatmap data to fill gaps.

    Our core hypothesis—journey disruption—proved difficult to isolate at first. Milk was located deep in the cold beverage aisle, toward the rear of the store. But many shoppers walked straight to the cash register, particularly those making quick trips. Capturing this behavior required us to piece together indirect indicators: basket contents, directional flow, and even what aisles shoppers paused in.

    When directly asked, some participants offered socially acceptable reasons for their choices (“I always shop the whole store,” “I buy the brand my kids like”), which conflicted with their actual observed behaviors. We mitigated this by asking about shopping routines, not specific purchases, and using reflective prompts like: “When was the last time you bought milk here?”

    RESULTS & BUSINESS OUTCOMES

  • We detected a post-pandemic trend toward price shopping
  • With trade, sales, and marketing teams: - relocation of a portion of the portfolio
  • Tracking of new promotions and campaigns.
  • Average ticket increased 7%
  • Cold Beverage sales increased 14%: profitability comes from the shopping experience.
  • Milk sales increased 12% in the first month after the campaign.
  • In-store journeys were optimized, with 30% of shoppers now passing through the cold beverage section.
  • Sales of private-label milk SKUs increased 15% in the first quarter after the campaign.
  • Byzzer data showed a 6% increase in private-label milk sales share relative to national brands.
