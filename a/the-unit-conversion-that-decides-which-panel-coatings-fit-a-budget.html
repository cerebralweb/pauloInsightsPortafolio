THE UNIT CONVERSION THAT DECIDES WHICH PANEL COATINGS FIT A BUDGET

In this article ❧ Finishing decisions on hardware rarely come down to a single surface. Teams pick a coating per panel, and each coating may be quoted in a different unit, which makes the budget comparison easy to get wrong. The fix is to convert every area to a common basis, price each option, then test each pairing against the limit. This problem shows how a short chain of area, conversion, and addition can model a real spending decision, and how Python can turn that logic into a reusable tool.

The Problem

A hardware team finishes two enclosure panels for a new edge server unit. One square panel, one meter per side, mounts the main board. A second panel, 200 centimeters wide by 100 centimeters high, mounts the cooling assembly. The team has a budget of $38,000 to coat both panels. Two coatings are available. A conformal lacquer protects against moisture and costs $1.2 per square centimeter. A thermal white coating improves heat dissipation and costs $14,000 per square meter.

Which coating combinations let the team stay within budget?

  • a) Lacquer-White and White-White
  • b) White-Lacquer and Lacquer-Lacquer
  • c) White-white or Lacquer-white
  • d) Lacquer and white and White and white
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) White-Lacquer and Lacquer-Lacquer ✅

Math Solution

Convert each panel to a common basis, then multiply area by each rate to price the options. Add the two panel costs for every pairing and compare to the budget. Only the two lowest totals, $38,000 and $36,000, stay at or under the limit.

One square meter holds 10,000 square centimeters, so the unit you convert to controls the whole comparison.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Square panel area: 10,000 cm² and 1 m²
  2. Cooling panel area: 20,000 cm² and 2 m²
  3. Square panel lacquer: 10,000 × 1.2 = $12,000
  4. Square panel white: 1 × 14,000 = $14,000
  5. Cooling panel lacquer: 20,000 × 1.2 = $24,000
  6. Cooling panel white: 2 × 14,000 = $28,000
  7. White then Lacquer: 14,000 + 24,000 = $38,000, fits
  8. Lacquer then Lacquer: 12,000 + 24,000 = $36,000, fits
  9. Lacquer then White: 12,000 + 28,000 = $40,000, over
  10. White then White: 14,000 + 28,000 = $42,000, over

Historical Background

Estimating coating cost by surface area is old practice in manufacturing and construction, where painters and platers have long priced work by the square unit. The trouble has always been mixed units, a problem the metric system was built to ease when France defined the meter and a decimal scale in the 1790s. That base ten structure is why a square meter converts cleanly to ten thousand square centimeters, which keeps area based estimates fast and consistent across suppliers.

Python Version

This script prices every coating mix for any two panel sizes and two rates, then flags which mixes fit a budget, so it works far beyond the original numbers.

# What this does. Works out which coating mixes fit the budget.
# Where it came from. Two enclosure panels for an edge server, each gets one coating.
# The math idea. Area times a cost rate, then compare each total to the budget.
# You type in. The two panel sizes, the budget, and the two coating rates.
# It prints. Each coating mix with its total and whether it fits.

# This function does all the math and returns every mix.
def coating_plan(side_m, w_cm, h_cm, lacquer_per_cm2, white_per_m2, budget):
    # Area of the square panel in square centimeters. One meter is 100 centimeters.
    panel1_cm2 = (side_m * 100) * (side_m * 100)
    # Same panel in square meters.
    panel1_m2 = side_m * side_m
    # Area of the second panel in square centimeters.
    panel2_cm2 = w_cm * h_cm
    # Same panel in square meters.
    panel2_m2 = (w_cm / 100) * (h_cm / 100)

    # Cost to coat each panel with each option.
    p1_lacquer = panel1_cm2 * lacquer_per_cm2
    p1_white = panel1_m2 * white_per_m2
    p2_lacquer = panel2_cm2 * lacquer_per_cm2
    p2_white = panel2_m2 * white_per_m2

    # The two choices for each panel.
    panel1_options = [("lacquer", p1_lacquer), ("white", p1_white)]
    panel2_options = [("lacquer", p2_lacquer), ("white", p2_white)]

    # Try every pairing, one coating per panel.
    plans = []
    for name1, cost1 in panel1_options:
        for name2, cost2 in panel2_options:
            total = cost1 + cost2
            # A mix fits when its total is at or under the budget.
            plans.append((name1 + " then " + name2, total, total <= budget))
    return plans

# Make money read cleanly. Whole when it can be, else two decimals.
def tidy(amount):
    return int(amount) if float(amount).is_integer() else round(amount, 2)

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find which coating mixes fit your budget.")

# Ask for the square panel side in meters.
side_m = float(input("Square panel side in meters? "))

# Ask for the second panel width in centimeters.
w_cm = float(input("Second panel width in centimeters? "))

# Ask for the second panel height in centimeters.
h_cm = float(input("Second panel height in centimeters? "))

# Ask for the budget.
budget = float(input("Budget in dollars? "))

# Ask for the lacquer rate per square centimeter.
lacquer_per_cm2 = float(input("Lacquer cost per square centimeter? "))

# Ask for the white rate per square meter.
white_per_m2 = float(input("White cost per square meter? "))

# Do the math for every mix.
plans = coating_plan(side_m, w_cm, h_cm, lacquer_per_cm2, white_per_m2, budget)

# Print each mix with words that fit the scenario.
print("Edge server panels. Pairs read as square panel then second panel.")
for label, total, fits in plans:
    status = "fits" if fits else "over"
    print(label + "  total " + str(tidy(total)) + "  " + status)

Run it in a terminal, type the six values, and it lists each mix with its total and a fits or over tag. A second version uses Python's itertools to build the pairings, which keeps the code short if more panels or coatings are added.

# Where it came from. Two enclosure panels for an edge server, each gets one coating.
# The math idea. Area times a cost rate, then compare each total to the budget.
# You type in. The two panel sizes, the budget, and the two coating rates.
# It prints. Each coating mix with its total and whether it fits.

# This tool pairs the options for us, so the code stays short if panels are added.
from itertools import product

# This function does all the math and returns every mix.
def coating_plan(side_m, w_cm, h_cm, lacquer_per_cm2, white_per_m2, budget):
    # Areas in both units. One meter is 100 centimeters.
    panel1_cm2 = (side_m * 100) * (side_m * 100)
    panel1_m2 = side_m * side_m
    panel2_cm2 = w_cm * h_cm
    panel2_m2 = (w_cm / 100) * (h_cm / 100)

    panel2_options = [("lacquer", panel2_cm2 * lacquer_per_cm2), ("white", panel2_m2 * white_per_m2)]

    plans = []
    for (name1, cost1), (name2, cost2) in product(panel1_options, panel2_options):

        # A mix fits when its total is at or under the budget.

# Panel Coating Budget Check

This skill takes two panel sizes, a budget, and two coating rates priced in different units, then works out the cost of every coating mix and reports which ones stay within budget. Each panel gets exactly one coating. The input is six numbers typed by the user. The output is a short list naming every mix with its total and a fits or over tag.

---

## Dependencies

None beyond Python's standard library. No external packages are needed.

---

## Inputs

- **Square panel side** in meters. The length of one side of the square panel.
- **Second panel width** in centimeters.
- **Second panel height** in centimeters.
- **Budget** in dollars. The most the user can spend on both panels.
- **Lacquer rate** in dollars per square centimeter.
- **White rate** in dollars per square meter.

---

## Steps

### Step 1. Collect the inputs

Ask for the six values listed above. Treat all of them as decimals, since sizes and money may not be whole numbers.

### Step 2. Find each panel area in both units

The two coatings are priced in different units, so each panel needs an area in square centimeters and an area in square meters.

For the square panel, multiply the side by itself to get the area in square meters. Convert the side to centimeters first by multiplying by 100, then multiply that by itself to get the area in square centimeters.

For the second panel, multiply width by height to get the area in square centimeters. Divide each side by 100 before multiplying to get the area in square meters.

### Step 3. Price each coating on each panel

For each panel, work out two prices. The lacquer price is the square centimeter area times the lacquer rate. The white price is the square meter area times the white rate. This produces four numbers, a lacquer price and a white price for each of the two panels.

### Step 4. List every coating mix

Each panel takes exactly one coating, so pair one option from the first panel with one option from the second. There are four pairings. For each pairing, add the two panel prices to get a total. Label each pairing by the square panel coating first and the second panel coating next.

### Step 5. Mark each mix against the budget

A mix fits when its total is at or under the budget. Mark any mix with a total above the budget as over.

### Step 6. Report the result

Print one short line naming the scenario, then list every mix with its total and a fits or over tag. Show whole dollar amounts without decimals when the value is whole, otherwise round money to two decimals. State that pairs read as the square panel coating first and the second panel coating next.
