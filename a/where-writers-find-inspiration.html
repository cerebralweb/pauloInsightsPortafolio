On Inspiration (Where Writers Look for Inspiration)

C. L. Palacios

In this article ❧ I argue that myths and marketing research are the main sources of inspiration for romance and fantasy writers..

Mythology. It is everywhere. It is on the NFL, on the cereal box, and, of course, on movies and books. Mythology is a dramatic account of our fears and desires. Tony the Tiger is an account of the gods that cure deseases with sugar and corn, just as a football game is an account of ancient wars.

Myths are universal. We can find them in any culture in human history. 

Myths

For a long time, myths have alleviated fear through storytelling. Some painful feelings come from wars, others from starvation, sickness, and drought, and many more from fantasies over erotism and richness. Fiction is an evolution of mythology (I include here movies and video games). 

How do we know contemporary fiction is an update of ancient mythology? We compare the themes and parts of typical fiction with mythology, for example, Forest Gump and the Odyssey. Forrest is in trouble because of illness and death; Ulysses is in trouble because of disease, death, and love. Both heroes depart for a long and turbulent trip. And the structure? Three acts. Step 1: problem for a hero; step 2: an adventure (a trip); step 3: climax (each hero returns somehow home).

Death is the supreme fear. Each ancient myth, movie, or novel showcases a hero that has to change because change represents that nothing dies but transforms. Each change summons a symbolic cycle of death and birth.

The Swiss psychoanalyst Carl Jung understood why we pay good money to watch a movie or read a book––modern versions of myths. Myths are stories run by archetypes: the hero, the destroyer, the orphan, etcetera. The collective unconscious of humans has created these archetypes, the same that perform in our dreams. We awake and barely remember what we dreamt (or we miss the tread). In the meantime, writers and producers are making movies, series, video games, and books based on the same source: the archetypes shared by dreams, myths, and entertainment. 

Religion and psychology have been other sources of relief. Religion detaches the person from desire (sex) and Thanatos (hostility and death). Psychology helps individuals release themselves from unreal terrors and ambivalent attractions caused by unconsciously tangled desires and hostilities (so they feel more pleasure with real problems and opportunities like wars, religions, erotism, businesses, pastimes, and household tasks!!). Joseph Campbell stated that myth, psychology, and religion prepare men for enlightenment.

Insights and Marketing Research

But let's be honest. Marketing research is a helpful source, too. Like other products, authors and publishers can advertise books and sold based on empirical knowledge and a qualified interpretation of consumers' behavior. Yes, we are far from the romantic conceptions of writers as pure artists, hearing solely the muse of their desires. A book following the sentimental road increases the chances of low numbers in sales; the writer listening to the muses of marketing research (not the voices of agents) is more likely to land an outsold book. Where should you look for insights? I do it in two places: WARC and YPulse. Sometimes, I like to read Teen Vogue, too, since I know many females may feel identified with that kind of publication.

No that you are in a different department with marketing research. Any product (food, clothing, technology, or music) is a new account of human culture, and any company talks about its product as a mythological hero.

And Literature?

Literature and art can still be convenient sources for our books. The right way is to think of them as archives of techniques and aesthetic solutions that can be applied to actual problems by savvy writers.
