DIVIDE BEFORE YOU ADD: THE FRACTIONAL MATH BEHIND AGENDA PLANNING

In this article ❧ Meetings are a fixed resource, and the arithmetic of how agenda time gets divided is one of the most routine — and most overlooked — calculations in business planning. Before a director walks into a room, she can verify in seconds whether her fractional allocations are realistic or whether they silently consume most of the clock. This problem shows how the divide-then-scale mental model turns fractional shares of a fixed whole into concrete minutes, and how that same logic extends to Python and a reusable Claude skill.

The Problem

A marketing director has a 60-minute campaign review meeting. She allocates 2/5 of that time to analyzing paid media performance and 1/4 to reviewing organic content results.

How many minutes did she spend in total on these two activities?

  • a) 9.23
  • b) 4
  • c) 39
  • d) 3.9
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: c) 39 minutes ✅

Math Solution

The key move is divide first, then scale. For each fraction, divide the total meeting time by the denominator to find one equal part, then multiply by the numerator to reach the target share. The two results are then added together to give the combined agenda time consumed.

The mental math anchor for 2/5 of 60 is the fact that 12 × 5 = 60, so one-fifth is 12 — making two-fifths simply 12 × 2 = 24. Quarters of 60 are always clean: 15, 30, 45, 60.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Convert total time: 1 hour = 60 minutes → working unit is 60
  2. Paid media block — 2/5 of 60: 60 ÷ 5 = 12 (one-fifth) → 12 × 2 = 24 minutes
  3. Organic content block — 1/4 of 60: 60 ÷ 4 = 15 minutes
  4. Combine both blocks: 24 + 15 = 39 minutes
  5. Conclusion: The director uses 39 of her 60 minutes on these two agenda items, leaving 21 minutes for discussion and wrap-up.

Historical Background

The operation of finding a fractional part of a given quantity — divide by the denominator, then multiply by the numerator — was formalized in the 9th century by the Persian mathematician al-Khwārizmī, whose treatise on arithmetic made rational operations available to merchants, land surveyors, and inheritance lawyers across the Islamic world. His framework turned fractions from an abstract concept into a practical computation tool: given any measurable whole, any named part of it could be extracted through two integer steps. The rule has not changed in twelve centuries. What has changed is the scale: a marketing director allocating meeting time is performing exactly the same arithmetic that a medieval merchant used to divide a shipment into fractional consignments.

Python Version

The script below generalises the problem: it accepts any total meeting duration and returns the combined agenda time consumed by the two fixed fractional blocks (2/5 and 1/4).

def agenda_time(total, n1, d1, n2, d2):
    return total * n1 / d1 + total * n2 / d2

t = int(input("Total meeting time (min): "))
result = agenda_time(t, 2, 5, 1, 4)
print(f"Agenda time used: {int(result) if result == int(result) else result} min")

Run it in Windows Terminal by entering python, pasting the block, pressing Enter, and supplying the meeting duration — it returns the combined minutes consumed as a whole number whenever the result is exact.

# Agenda Time Calculator

This skill takes a total meeting duration and two fractional time allocations, computes
how many minutes each agenda block consumes, adds them together, and reports the combined
time used. It is designed for marketing, operations, or project teams that assign portions
of a meeting to specific activities and need to confirm the total before the meeting starts.

---

## Dependencies

List any libraries or tools Claude needs to have available:
- No external libraries required. Works with Python's built-in `fractions` module
  if exact rational arithmetic is preferred over standard floating-point division.

---

## Inputs

- **Total meeting time**: A whole number representing the full meeting duration
  in minutes (e.g., 60).
- **First agenda fraction**: The share of meeting time assigned to the first
  activity, expressed as a numerator and denominator (e.g., 2 out of 5).
- **Second agenda fraction**: The share assigned to the second activity,
  expressed the same way (e.g., 1 out of 4).

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Receive the Total Meeting Time

Ask the user for the total meeting duration in minutes. Expect a whole number. Store it
as the working base for all subsequent calculations.

### Step 2 — Apply the First Fraction

Divide the total meeting time by the denominator of the first fraction to find one equal
part. Multiply that part by the numerator to get the number of minutes assigned to the
first agenda block.

### Step 3 — Apply the Second Fraction

Repeat the same process for the second fraction: divide the total time by its denominator,
then multiply by its numerator to get the minutes for the second agenda block.

### Step 4 — Sum the Two Blocks

Add the minutes from Step 2 and Step 3 to produce the total time consumed by both
agenda activities.

### Step 5 — Display the Result

Print the combined total with a short, plain-English label identifying it as the time
used across both agenda blocks. If the result is a whole number, display it without
a decimal point.
