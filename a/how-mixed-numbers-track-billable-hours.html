ADD THE FRACTIONS FIRST: HOW MIXED NUMBERS TRACK BILLABLE HOURS (AND WHICH ANCIENT NUMERAL SYSTEM MAKES IT ALL WORK)

In this article ❧ Consultants, project managers, and analysts log time across multiple sessions every week. Whether the context is client billing, sprint tracking, or compliance reporting, the ability to determine how much time remains in a planned block — when prior sessions are recorded as fractions of an hour — is a practical operational skill. This problem illustrates how adding and subtracting mixed numbers models a real time-tracking scenario, and how Python and a reusable Claude skill can turn that logic into a repeatable, always-ready tool.

The Problem

A project consultant needs to log 6½ billable hours this weekend to meet a client deliverable milestone. On Friday, she logged 1 hour and 15 minutes reviewing documentation. On Saturday, she logged 2 hours and 2/3 conducting stakeholder interviews.

How many hours does she still need to log on Sunday to reach the milestone?

  • a) 150 minutes
  • b) 2 hours and 7/12
  • c) 27/12
  • d) 2 hours and 9/12
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) 2 hours and 7/12 ✅

Math Solution

The key move is converting every time value to a fraction over a common denominator of 12, adding the logged hours, then subtracting from the target. The number 12 appears naturally because it is the least common multiple of 4 (from 15 minutes = ¼ hour) and 3 (from the 2/3 session). A faster but more error-prone path is to add hours and minutes separately — reaching 3 hours and 55 minutes logged — then subtract from 6 hours 30 minutes to get 2 hours 35 minutes, and convert 35 minutes to 7/12 by observing that 60 divides into 12 segments of 5 minutes, of which 35 contains 7.

The critical insight is that 60 minutes divides evenly into 12 equal segments of 5 minutes each — a structural property of the Babylonian base-60 system — which is why 35 remaining minutes translates cleanly to 7/12 of an hour with no remainder.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived (Method A — systematic fraction conversion):

  1. Convert Friday hours: 1 hour 15 min = 1¼ = 5/4
  2. Convert Saturday hours: 2 and 2/3 = 8/3
  3. Find common denominator (LCD of 4 and 3 = 12): 5/4 → 15/12  |  8/3 → 32/12
  4. Add logged hours: 15/12 + 32/12 = 47/12
  5. Convert target to twelfths: 6½ = 13/2 = 78/12
  6. Subtract: 78/12 − 47/12 = 31/12
  7. Convert to mixed number: 31 ÷ 12 = 2 remainder 7 → 2 and 7/12 hours

Historical Background

The reason this problem resolves so cleanly in twelfths traces directly to Babylon. Around 2000 BCE, Mesopotamian scribes adopted a base-60 positional numeral system — the sexagesimal system — in large part because 60 has an unusually high number of divisors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, and 60. This made it possible to express most common fractions as whole subdivisions without remainder, which was essential for astronomical calculation, land measurement, and timekeeping. When Hellenistic astronomers later formalized the 60-minute hour and 60-second minute, they were borrowing a computational convenience that Babylonian accountants had relied on for a millennium. Every time a consultant converts 15 minutes to ¼ hour or 40 minutes to ⅔ hour, they are using arithmetic that was designed in ancient Mesopotamia to make exactly these kinds of fractional relationships come out whole.

Our system (base-10) uses 10 digits (0–9) and resets every power of 10: ones, tens, hundreds. Every time you exhaust 10 combinations at one position, you increment the position to the left by 1 and reset the right position to 0. So 9 → 10, 99 → 100, 999 → 1000. Every multi-digit number is just a sum of digits multiplied by their positional power of 10: 347 = (3×100) + (4×10) + (7×1). The digits never change — always 0–9 — but their position determines their weight.

Base-60 uses 60 distinct symbols before resetting. The practical payoff: 60 divides evenly by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, and 30. Base-10 only divides evenly by 2 and 5.That's why splitting an hour into thirds (20 min), quarters (15 min), or sixths (10 min) always comes out whole — and splitting a dollar into thirds doesn't.

In base-10, you count: 1, 2, 3 … all the way to 9, and when you run out of single digits you borrow a slot on the left: 10, 11, 12 … 99, then 100, and so on. Base-60 works exactly the same way — but instead of running out at 9, you don't borrow that left slot until you've counted all the way to 59. So you have 60 individual symbols (the Babylonians used combinations of two wedge marks pressed into clay to build them). Only after you hit 59 does a "1" appear in the left slot — meaning "one full group of sixty." The practical result: where we write "1:30" to mean one hour and thirty minutes, a Babylonian would write it as two symbols — one mark meaning "one sixty" and another meaning "thirty." Same idea, bigger bucket before the reset.

In the original Babylonian cuneiform system, they only had two symbols:

  • 𒁹 (a narrow vertical wedge) = 1
  • 𒌋 (a wider corner wedge) = 10

So they built numbers by combining them, exactly like tally marks:

Value Symbol
1 𒁹
10 𒌋
59 𒌋𒌋𒌋𒌋𒌋𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹𒁹 (five 10s + nine 1s)
60 𒁹 (a single "1" — but now in the left position)

That last row is the key. "One sixty" looks identical to "one" — the only thing that distinguishes them is position, just like how the "1" in 10 looks like the "1" in 1, but sits one slot to the left.

This is also why Babylonian math was occasionally ambiguous — they had no zero to hold an empty position for much of their history.

In base-10, every multi-digit number is a sum of digits multiplied by their positional power of 10. The digit itself never changes — it is always 0–9 — but where it sits determines what it is worth.

Position Power Value Digit Contribution
Thousands 10³ 1,000 3 3 × 1,000 = 3,000
Hundreds 10² 100 4 4 × 100 = 400
Tens 10¹ 10 7 7 × 10 = 70
Ones 10⁰ 1 2 2 × 1 = 2

Total: 3,000 + 400 + 70 + 2 = 3,472. A zero in any column contributes nothing to the total — but it must stay there as a placeholder so every other digit keeps its correct position.

Python Version

The script below accepts a billable target and two logged session times — each entered as a whole number of hours plus a fraction given as numerator and denominator — and returns the hours still needed for the third session. Using Python's built-in fractions.Fraction type gives exact rational arithmetic with automatic simplification, eliminating the manual GCD step and any risk of floating-point rounding error.

from fractions import Fraction

def hours_remaining(target, day1, day2):
    logged = day1 + day2
    remaining = target - logged
    return remaining

print("Billable Hours Tracker — How much time is left for the final session?\n")

target_w = int(input("Total billable target — whole hours: "))
target_n = int(input("Total billable target — fraction numerator: "))
target_d = int(input("Total billable target — fraction denominator: "))

day1_w = int(input("\nDay 1 logged — whole hours: "))
day1_n = int(input("Day 1 logged — fraction numerator: "))
day1_d = int(input("Day 1 logged — fraction denominator: "))

day2_w = int(input("\nDay 2 logged — whole hours: "))
day2_n = int(input("Day 2 logged — fraction numerator: "))
day2_d = int(input("Day 2 logged — fraction denominator: "))

target  = Fraction(target_w) + Fraction(target_n, target_d)
day1    = Fraction(day1_w)   + Fraction(day1_n,   day1_d)
day2    = Fraction(day2_w)   + Fraction(day2_n,   day2_d)

result  = hours_remaining(target, day1, day2)
whole   = int(result)
frac    = result - whole

if frac == 0:
    print(f"\nBillable hours still needed on Day 3: {whole} hours")
else:
    print(f"\nBillable hours still needed on Day 3: {whole} and {frac.numerator}/{frac.denominator} hours")

Run it in Windows Terminal by typing python, pasting the code, and pressing Enter; using the original problem values (target: 6, 1, 2 — Day 1: 1, 1, 4 — Day 2: 2, 2, 3) produces Billable hours still needed on Day 3: 2 and 7/12 hours.

# Billable Hours Tracker

This skill takes a total time target and two prior session logs — each expressed as a whole
number of hours plus a fractional part — and calculates how many hours still need to be logged
in a third session to meet the target. It produces a clean mixed-number result (e.g., "2 and
7/12 hours") with brief business-context text. It is designed to run interactively, prompting
the user for each value one at a time.

---

## Dependencies

- No third-party libraries required.
- **Option A** uses only basic arithmetic and a greatest-common-divisor helper.
- **Option B** uses `fractions.Fraction` from the Python standard library — available in all Python 3 installations without installation.

---

## Inputs

- **Billable target — whole hours**: The full-hour portion of the total time goal (e.g., `6` for a 6½-hour target).
- **Billable target — fraction numerator**: The numerator of the fractional hour portion of the target (e.g., `1` for ½).
- **Billable target — fraction denominator**: The denominator of the fractional hour portion of the target (e.g., `2` for ½).
- **Day 1 logged — whole hours**: Full hours logged in the first session.
- **Day 1 logged — fraction numerator**: Numerator of the fractional part logged on Day 1.
- **Day 1 logged — fraction denominator**: Denominator of the fractional part logged on Day 1.
- **Day 2 logged — whole hours**: Full hours logged in the second session.
- **Day 2 logged — fraction numerator**: Numerator of the fractional part logged on Day 2.
- **Day 2 logged — fraction denominator**: Denominator of the fractional part logged on Day 2.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Open the session and prompt the user

Print a short opening line that frames the task in business language — for example:
*"Billable Hours Tracker — How much time is left for the final session?"*
Then collect the nine inputs one at a time, in this order: target whole hours, target fraction
numerator, target fraction denominator; Day 1 whole hours, fraction numerator, fraction
denominator; Day 2 whole hours, fraction numerator, fraction denominator. Use clear,
plain-language prompts for each entry.

### Step 2 — Convert each mixed number to a single fraction

For each of the three time values (target, Day 1, Day 2), combine the whole-hour part and the
fractional part into a single fraction. Multiply the whole hours by the denominator, add the
numerator, and keep the same denominator. This gives a single fraction for each value that
represents the total time as a ratio of parts to a common unit.

### Step 3 — Add the two session logs together

Add the Day 1 fraction and the Day 2 fraction. To add fractions with different denominators,
first find a common denominator by multiplying the two denominators together. Scale each
fraction's numerator accordingly, then add the numerators. The result is the total time already
logged, expressed as a single fraction.

### Step 4 — Subtract logged time from the target

Find a common denominator between the target fraction and the logged total fraction, then
subtract the logged total from the target. The result is the remaining time the consultant must
log on Day 3, expressed as a single fraction.

### Step 5 — Simplify the result

Reduce the resulting fraction to its lowest terms by dividing both the numerator and the
denominator by their greatest common divisor. To find the GCD, repeatedly replace the larger
value with the remainder of dividing the larger by the smaller until the remainder is zero;
the last non-zero value is the GCD.

### Step 6 — Convert the simplified fraction to a mixed number

Divide the simplified numerator by the simplified denominator using integer division to get
the whole-hour portion. The remainder becomes the new numerator, and the denominator stays the
same. This gives the final mixed-number result (e.g., 2 and 7/12).

### Step 7 — Print the result with business-context text

If the remainder is zero, print only the whole hours
(e.g., *"Billable hours still needed on Day 3: 3 hours"*).
Otherwise, print the mixed number
(e.g., *"Billable hours still needed on Day 3: 2 and 7/12 hours"*).
The output must include a short descriptive label — never print the number alone.

---

## Alternate Execution Path — Standard Library (Exact Rational Arithmetic)

When precision and code brevity are the priority, use the `Fraction` type
from Python's built-in `fractions` module instead of manual arithmetic.

- Build each time value by adding a whole-number Fraction to the fractional-part Fraction.
- Add Day 1 and Day 2 directly using the `+` operator.
- Subtract the logged total from the target using the `-` operator.
- The result is already fully reduced automatically — no GCD step is needed.
- Read the whole-hour portion by converting the result to an integer and subtracting it from the result.
- Print using the same mixed-number format described in Step 7.

This path eliminates the need for a manual GCD function, avoids floating-point rounding errors,
and produces identical output to Option A.

---

## Verification Case

To confirm the program is working correctly, run it with these values:

- Target: **6 whole hours, numerator 1, denominator 2** (= 6½)
- Day 1: **1 whole hour, numerator 1, denominator 4** (= 1¼ = 1 hour 15 minutes)
- Day 2: **2 whole hours, numerator 2, denominator 3** (= 2⅔)

Expected output: **Billable hours still needed on Day 3: 2 and 7/12 hours**
