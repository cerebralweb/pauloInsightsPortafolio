95 LICENSES, ONE RATIO RULE, ONE CLEAN PROFIT FIGURE

In this article ❧ Margin questions often arrive backward. A team knows the money that came in and the count of units sold, but not how that money split between what each unit cost and what each unit earned. A fixed relationship between cost and profit turns this fog into a clean division, because it lets you treat every sale as a stack of equal shares. This problem shows how a single ratio rule recovers the profit on one unit from a lone total, and how Python turns that rule into a tool you reuse on any future sale.

The Problem

A small team resells cloud monitoring licenses. In one month they sell 95 licenses for a combined 230,085 pesos. For each license, the acquisition cost is twice the profit earned on that license.

What is the profit per license?

  • a) 807.30
  • b) 80.73
  • c) 8.07
  • d) 403.7
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer a) 807.30 ✅

Math Solution

Each sale divides into three equal shares, two for cost and one for profit, because cost equals twice profit. Divide the total 230,085 by 95 units to get the revenue on one license, then divide that by 3 to value the single profit share.

When two amounts hold a fixed ratio, you can treat the whole as a sum of equal shares and solve it with one division.

Step by Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Define the unknown. Let p be the profit per license.
  2. Express the cost. Cost per license is 2p.
  3. Express unit revenue. Revenue per license is 2p plus p, so 3p.
  4. Express total revenue. 95 units give 95 times 3p, so 285p.
  5. Set up the equation. 285p equals 230,085.
  6. Solve. p equals 230,085 divided by 285, so 807.3158.
  7. Round to money. 807.32, and the nearest choice is a) 807.30.

Historical Background

Splitting a shared sum by fixed proportion is one of the oldest tasks in commerce. Medieval merchants in the Italian trading cities divided the returns of a joint venture among partners by agreed shares, a method their account books called the rule of company. Leonardo of Pisa, the merchant son known later as Fibonacci, gave long passages of his 1202 Liber Abaci to these partnership and profit splitting cases, and the merchant schools of Florence and Venice taught them for centuries to clerks who would never write an equation.

Python Version

This script recovers the profit on one unit from a total sale figure under any fixed cost to profit ratio, so it works on this license example and on any future one.

# What this does: works out the profit on each license from total sales.
# Where it came from: a team resold licenses, and each unit's cost was a fixed multiple of its profit.
# The math idea: split each sale into equal parts, then find the value of one part.
# You type in: the total revenue, the number of licenses, and how many parts the cost equals.
# It prints: the profit earned on one license.

# This function does the math.
def profit_per_unit(total_revenue, units, cost_parts):
    # Each sale is the cost parts plus one profit part.
    total_parts = cost_parts + 1
    # Revenue on one license.
    revenue_each = total_revenue / units
    # One part is the profit. Split the unit revenue into the parts.
    return revenue_each / total_parts

# Tell the reader what we will figure out.
print("Let's find the profit earned on each license.")

# Ask for the total revenue from all licenses.
total_revenue = float(input("Total revenue: "))

# Ask for how many licenses were sold.
units = float(input("Number of licenses: "))

# Ask how many parts the cost equals, when profit is one part.
cost_parts = float(input("Cost parts per license (profit is 1 part): "))

# Do the math.
result = profit_per_unit(total_revenue, units, cost_parts)

# Round money to two decimals.
result = round(result, 2)

# Print the answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Profit per license:", result)

Run it in a terminal, enter 230085, then 95, then 2, and it prints 807.32, the profit on one license.

# Profit Per Unit From a Fixed Cost Ratio

This skill finds the profit earned on one unit when every sale splits into cost and profit in a fixed ratio. It takes the total revenue from all units, the number of units sold, and how many parts the cost equals when profit is one part. It returns the profit per unit. The method treats each sale as a set of equal parts, finds the revenue on one unit, then divides that into parts to value the single profit part.

---

## Dependencies

None. Basic arithmetic only.

---

## Inputs

- **Total revenue**: the combined money from all units sold. A number, may include decimals.
- **Number of units**: how many units were sold. A number.
- **Cost parts per unit**: how many parts the cost equals when profit is one part. For a cost twice the profit, this is 2.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Collect the three inputs

Ask for the total revenue, the number of units, and the cost parts per unit. Treat all three as numbers that may carry decimals.

### Step 2 — Find the total number of parts

Add one to the cost parts. The cost parts plus the single profit part give the total parts in each sale. For a cost twice the profit, that is 2 plus 1, so 3 parts.

### Step 3 — Find the revenue on one unit

Divide the total revenue by the number of units. This gives the money from a single sale.

### Step 4 — Find the profit on one unit

Divide the revenue on one unit by the total parts. One part is the profit, so this value is the profit per unit.

### Step 5 — Round and report

Round the result to two decimals for money. Report it with text that names the scenario, e.g. "Profit per unit: 807.32".
