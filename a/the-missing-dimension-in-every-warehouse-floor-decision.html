THE MISSING DIMENSION IN EVERY WAREHOUSE FLOOR DECISION

In this article ❧ Space decisions hinge on numbers that a listing often omits. A team weighing one floor against another must recover the measurement that was not handed, because area and fencing drive both storage capacity and cost. This problem shows how area, a square root, and a perimeter ratio each return a missing dimension, and how Python turns that work into a reusable tool.

The Problem

A logistics firm is choosing one warehouse from three available units. The first unit has a rectangular floor with an area of 3,600 square meters, and its width is 40 meters. The second unit has a square floor with an area of 3,600 square meters. The third unit has a rectangular floor with a fence perimeter of 3,600 meters, and its length is 5 times its width.

What is the length of the first unit, the side of the second, and the width of the third, all in meters?

  • a) 40, 90, 600
  • b) 90, 40, 300
  • c) 90, 60, 300
  • d) 40, 60, 30
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: c) 90, 60, 300 ✅

Math Solution

Unit one length comes from area divided by width, so 3,600 over 40 gives 90. Unit two side is the square root of 3,600, which is 60, and unit three width follows from 12 times width equals 3,600, so the width is 300.

One area, one square root, and one ratio each unlock a different missing side.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amount is derived:

  1. Unit one length: 3,600 / 40 = 90 meters
  2. Unit two side: square root of 3,600 = 60 meters
  3. Unit three setup: 2 times (5W plus W) = 12W
  4. Unit three width: 3,600 / 12 = 300 meters

Why Three Figures

The problem poses three figures because each unit hides a different relation and needs a different inverse step. Unit one gives the area and one side, so dividing recovers the other side. Unit two gives the area as a side multiplied by itself, so the square root recovers that side. Unit three gives no area at all. It gives the distance around the floor plus a ratio between the sides, so substitution and division recover the width. The set checks three ideas. Area as a plain product, area as a square, and perimeter as a sum.

The Algebra Behind Unit Three

Let W stand for the width. The length is 5 times the width, so the length is 5W. Perimeter is twice the length plus twice the width, which is 2(L + W). Substitute 5W for L. This gives 2(5W + W). Add inside the bracket first. 5W + W is 6W, so the expression is 2(6W). Multiply the 2 through. 2 times 6W is 12W. The perimeter is therefore 12W. Set it equal to the given perimeter. 12W = 3600. Divide both sides by 12. W = 300. The width is 300 meters, and the length is 5 times 300, which is 1500 meters.

Historical Background

Land and floor measurement sit among the oldest uses of arithmetic. Babylonian scribes computed square roots on clay tablets more than 3,500 years ago, and Egyptian rope stretchers laid out fields by area and perimeter long before algebra had symbols. The same reasoning still prices warehouse leases and plots factory floors today.

Python Version

The script asks for each known measurement and returns the missing length, side, and width, so it serves any floor that fits these three shapes.

# What this does: finds the missing measurement for three warehouse floors.
# Where it came from: a logistics firm compared three units before leasing one.
# The math idea: area is length times width, a square side is the root of its area, and perimeter is twice the length plus width.
# You type in: unit one area and width, unit two area, unit three perimeter and the ratio of length to width.
# It prints: the missing length, side, and width for the three units in meters.

# This function finds a side when you know the area and the other side.
def length_from_area(area, width):
    # Divide the area by the known side to get the missing side.
    return area / width

# This function finds the side of a square floor from its area.
def square_side(area):
    # The side is the number that times itself gives the area.
    return area ** 0.5

# This function finds the width when length is a set number of times the width.
def width_from_perimeter(perimeter, ratio):
    # Perimeter is twice (length plus width), and length is ratio times width.
    # So perimeter is twice width times (ratio plus one). Undo that to get width.
    return perimeter / (2 * (ratio + 1))

# Tell the reader what we will work out.
print("Let's size three warehouse floors.")

# Ask for unit one area and width.
unit1_area = float(input("Unit one floor area in square meters: "))
unit1_width = float(input("Unit one width in meters: "))

# Ask for unit two area.
unit2_area = float(input("Unit two floor area in square meters: "))

# Ask for unit three perimeter and the length to width ratio.
unit3_perimeter = float(input("Unit three fence perimeter in meters: "))
unit3_ratio = float(input("Unit three length divided by width: "))

# Do the math for each unit.
unit1_length = length_from_area(unit1_area, unit1_width)
unit2_side = square_side(unit2_area)
unit3_width = width_from_perimeter(unit3_perimeter, unit3_ratio)

# Show whole numbers without a trailing decimal when they come out even.
unit1_length = int(unit1_length) if unit1_length.is_integer() else unit1_length
unit2_side = int(unit2_side) if unit2_side.is_integer() else unit2_side
unit3_width = int(unit3_width) if unit3_width.is_integer() else unit3_width

# Print each answer with words that fit the scenario.
print("Unit one length in meters:", unit1_length)
print("Unit two side in meters:", unit2_side)
print("Unit three width in meters:", unit3_width)

Run it in a terminal, enter the five numbers, and read the three results in meters.

# Warehouse Floor Sizer

This skill finds one missing measurement for up to three floors. It takes an area with one known side, a square area, and a perimeter paired with a length to width ratio, then returns the missing length, the square side, and the missing width in meters. Use it when a team compares spaces before leasing and needs the dimension that was not given.

---

## Dependencies

None. The skill uses plain arithmetic and a square root. No outside libraries are required.

---

## Inputs

- **Unit one area.** The floor area of the first space in square meters.
- **Unit one width.** The known side of the first space in meters.
- **Unit two area.** The floor area of the square second space in square meters.
- **Unit three perimeter.** The fence distance around the third space in meters.
- **Unit three ratio.** How many times longer the length is than the width for the third space.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Greet and collect the numbers

State that the goal is to size three floors. Ask for the five numbers in order. Read every number as a value that may carry decimals.

### Step 2 — Find the first length

Take the first floor area and divide it by the known width. The result is the missing length of the first space.

### Step 3 — Find the square side

Take the second floor area and find its square root, the value that times itself gives the area. The result is the side of the square second space.

### Step 4 — Find the third width

The third space has a length that is the ratio times its width. So its perimeter equals twice the width times the ratio plus one. Divide the perimeter by twice the ratio plus one to get the width.

### Step 5 — Tidy the results

For each answer, if the value comes out even, show it as a whole number with no trailing decimal. Otherwise leave the decimal in place.

### Step 6 — Report

Print three lines, one per space. Label each with its space and the word meters so the reader sees which measurement belongs to which floor.
