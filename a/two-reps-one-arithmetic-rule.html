26,104 TASKS, TWO REPS, ONE ARITHMETIC RULE

In this article ❧ Multi-step arithmetic is the skeleton of nearly every operational decision in business. When teams scale, pricing tiers diverge, and resource consumption compounds, the ability to chain simple operations — add, multiply, subtract — without reaching for a calculator separates analysts who lead the room from analysts who confirm after the meeting. This problem demonstrates how a realistic SaaS scenario transforms classroom arithmetic into a decision-support tool, and how two mental math strategies — fast estimation and systematic decomposition — serve different moments in the same workflow.

The Problem

Two enterprise software sales reps, Ana and David, close SaaS licenses every month. Ana closes 106 licenses; David closes 39 more than Ana. Each license requires 104 automated provisioning tasks to activate on the platform. How many provisioning tasks does the team run in total per month?

At the annual sales summit, Ana's licenses were each priced at $14,000, and David's licenses were each priced at $10,000 more than Ana's. What was the difference between their total monthly revenues?

  • a) 26,104 tasks and $207,584,000
  • b) 26,104 tasks and $1,996,000
  • c) 11,024 tasks and $199,600
  • d) 15,080 tasks and $1,996,000
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) 26,104 tasks and $1,996,000 ✅

Math Solution

To find total provisioning tasks, first compute each rep's license volume — David closes 106 + 39 = 145 licenses, giving a combined team total of 251 — then multiply by 104 tasks per license. For revenue, multiply each rep's volume by their respective unit price independently, then subtract to isolate the gap between them.

The critical move is recognizing that Ana and David carry different unit prices — revenues cannot be meaningfully compared until each volume is scaled by its own rate before the subtraction.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the final amounts are derived:

  1. David's license volume: 106 + 39 = 145 licenses
  2. Combined team volume: 106 + 145 = 251 licenses
  3. Total provisioning tasks: 251 × 104 = 26,104
  4. Ana's total revenue: 106 × $14,000 = $1,484,000
  5. David's unit price: $14,000 + $10,000 = $24,000
  6. David's total revenue: 145 × $24,000 = $3,480,000
  7. Revenue difference: $3,480,000 − $1,484,000 = $1,996,000

Mental Math

Two strategies reach the same answers — choose the one that fits the moment. Path A trades precision for speed; Path B trades speed for exactness. In a fast-moving business environment, knowing which path to run — and when — is itself a professional skill.

Path A — Round Aggressively, Get Close Fast

You never correct back. You accept a small error in exchange for a result you can compute in seconds, then use that result to screen the answer choices before investing in precision.

  • David's volume: 39 ≈ 40 → 106 + 40 = ~146 (actual: 145)
  • Team total: 106 ≈ 100 → 100 + 146 = ~246 (actual: 251)
  • Provisioning tasks: 246 ≈ 250, 104 ≈ 100 → 250 × 100 = ~25,000 (actual: 26,104)
  • Ana's revenue: 106 ≈ 100 → 100 × $14,000 = ~$1,400,000 (actual: $1,484,000)
  • David's unit price: $14,000 + $10,000 = $24,000 (exact, trivial)
  • David's revenue: 145 ≈ 150 → 150 × $24,000 = ~$3,600,000 (actual: $3,480,000)
  • Difference: $3,600,000 − $1,400,000 = ~$2,200,000 (actual: $1,996,000)

The estimates land within 5–10% of the exact values — close enough to eliminate wrong answer choices and catch an order-of-magnitude error before it becomes a decision. Path A is not a workaround for people who cannot do the arithmetic. It is the professional default in fast-paced environments: get a defensible estimate in five seconds, use it to orient, then invest in precision only if the decision requires it.

Path B — Split into Friendly Parts (Distributive Property)

  • Provisioning tasks (251 × 104): 251 × 100 + 251 × 4 = 25,100 + 1,004 = 26,104
  • Ana's revenue (106 × $14,000): 106 × $10,000 + 106 × $4,000 = $1,060,000 + $424,000 = $1,484,000
  • David's revenue (145 × $24,000): 145 × $20,000 + 145 × $4,000 = $2,900,000 + $580,000 = $3,480,000
  • Difference: $3,480,000 − $1,484,000 → subtract millions then thousands: 2,000,000 − 4,000 = $1,996,000

Path A tends to be faster when one factor is just above or below a round number and the goal is a quick sanity check. Path B is the right choice when precision is required and factors are irregular. Skilled mental calculators run Path A first to orient, then Path B to confirm.

Historical Background

The chained arithmetic at the heart of this problem — aggregate first, scale second, compare third — has roots in every commercial civilization that needed to plan resource allocation across multiple agents. Indian mathematicians of the Vedic period (roughly 1500–500 BCE) codified rapid approximation techniques in their sutras, treating rounding as a primary tool rather than a fallback; working with near-values was considered rigorous, not sloppy, when the goal was a fast and actionable result. A millennium later, al-Khwarizmi's 9th-century work in Baghdad gave the distributive property its algebraic form, demonstrating that any product could be decomposed and reassembled without changing its value — the formal foundation of Path B. Leonardo of Bonacci (Fibonacci) brought both traditions to European commercial life in his Liber Abaci (1202), demonstrating through merchant scenarios nearly identical in structure to this problem how chained operations could be executed rapidly with Hindu-Arabic numerals.

The 20th century added a productivity argument. Jakow Trachtenberg developed his speed-arithmetic system during imprisonment in Nazi concentration camps and published it in English as The Trachtenberg Speed System of Basic Mathematics (1960); it was subsequently adopted in business schools and military training programs as proof that fast, reliable mental calculation is a teachable skill, not an innate gift. Researcher Stanislas Dehaene, in The Number Sense (1997), documented that numerical intuition — the capacity to recognize when a result is in the right ballpark — is a distinct cognitive skill that degrades without regular practice, with direct consequences for professional judgment. Path A in this problem is precisely what Dehaene describes as calibrated numerical intuition: the default mode of experienced analysts working under time pressure.

Python Version

The script below generalizes the problem to any two-rep configuration — supply different volumes, provisioning rates, and prices, and it recomputes the full analysis in a single pass.

def saas_team_analysis(rep_a_licenses, extra_licenses, tasks_per_license,
                        rep_a_price, price_premium):
    rep_b_licenses = rep_a_licenses + extra_licenses
    total_licenses = rep_a_licenses + rep_b_licenses
    total_tasks = total_licenses * tasks_per_license
    rep_a_revenue = rep_a_licenses * rep_a_price
    rep_b_price = rep_a_price + price_premium
    rep_b_revenue = rep_b_licenses * rep_b_price
    revenue_diff = rep_b_revenue - rep_a_revenue
    return total_tasks, rep_a_revenue, rep_b_revenue, revenue_diff

print("--- SaaS Team: Provisioning Load & Revenue Comparison ---")
rep_a_licenses    = int(input("Rep A monthly licenses closed: "))
extra_licenses    = int(input("Extra licenses Rep B closes above Rep A: "))
tasks_per_license = int(input("Provisioning tasks per license: "))
rep_a_price       = float(input("Rep A unit price ($): "))
price_premium     = float(input("Rep B price premium above Rep A ($): "))

total_tasks, rev_a, rev_b, diff = saas_team_analysis(
    rep_a_licenses, extra_licenses, tasks_per_license, rep_a_price, price_premium
)

print(f"\nTotal provisioning tasks this month: {total_tasks:,}")
print(f"Rep A total revenue:  ${rev_a:,.2f}")
print(f"Rep B total revenue:  ${rev_b:,.2f}")
print(f"Revenue difference (Rep B over Rep A): ${diff:,.2f}")

Run it in Windows Terminal by typing python, pasting the block, pressing Enter twice, then supplying each value at the prompt — the script returns provisioning load and the full revenue comparison labeled and formatted.
