FROM FRACTIONS TO MINUTES: THE BILLING GAP CONSULTANTS CALCULATE DAILY

In this article ❧ Fractional billing is how most professional services firms track time — in tenths, quarters, or custom increments rather than whole hours. Knowing mid-day whether a billing target is within reach requires converting between units and working with unlike denominators, two skills that compound when time pressure is real. This article walks through the arithmetic step by step, shows how a Python script automates the calculation for any inputs, and packages the full logic as a reusable Claude skill.

The Problem

A management consultant tracks her billable hours using fractions of an hour. Her firm requires 2 billable hours per client per day. This morning she logged ¾ of an hour on the account, and after lunch she logged ⁷⁄₁₀ of an hour.

How many more minutes does she need to bill to meet the daily requirement?

  • a) 3
  • b) 33
  • c) 75
  • d) 7.5
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) 33 ✅

Math Solution

Convert the daily target to minutes (2 × 60 = 120), then convert each session. The morning session gives ¾ × 60 = 45 minutes. The afternoon session requires finding ⁷⁄₁₀ of 60 — which is where the mental math pays off.

Divide 60 into 10 equal parts: each part is 6 units. Take 7 of those parts: 7 × 6 = 42 minutes — so ⁷⁄₁₀ of an hour is exactly 42 minutes.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the remaining billable minutes are derived:

  1. Convert the daily target: 2 hours × 60 = 120 minutes
  2. Convert the morning session: ¾ × 60 = 45 minutes
  3. Convert the afternoon session — mental math dialog:
    "What is ⁷⁄₁₀ of 60?"
    "If I divide 60 into 10 equal parts, each part is 6 units."
    "I need 7 of those parts: 7 × 6 = 42."
    → ⁷⁄₁₀ of an hour = 42 minutes
  4. Sum the logged sessions: 45 + 42 = 87 minutes
  5. Compute the gap: 120 − 87 = 33 minutes
  6. Conclusion: The consultant needs to log 33 more minutes to meet her daily billable requirement — option b).

Historical Background

The base-60 number system — the reason 60 divides so cleanly into fractions like ⁷⁄₁₀ — was developed in Mesopotamia around 2000 BCE by Babylonian scribes who needed a number base divisible by many integers at once. Their choice of 60 was inherited by the Greeks, formalized in Hellenistic Alexandria, and eventually embedded in the 60-minute hour that governs billing systems today. When a consultant converts ⁷⁄₁₀ of an hour to exactly 42 minutes without a remainder, she is benefiting from an arithmetic decision made four thousand years ago.

The practice of adding fractions with unlike denominators — finding a common measure before combining parts — was already documented in cuneiform tablets from the Old Babylonian period and later systematized in medieval Islamic mathematics by scholars such as al-Khwārizmī, whose Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr formalized rules for rational arithmetic that remain the foundation of modern fraction operations.

Python Version

The script below uses the fractions.Fraction class from the Python standard library to perform exact arithmetic — no floating-point rounding — making it safe for billing and payroll contexts where a rounding error could compound across hundreds of entries.

from fractions import Fraction

def billable_gap(target_hours, frac1, frac2):
    target_minutes = Fraction(target_hours) * 60
    session1_minutes = frac1 * 60
    session2_minutes = frac2 * 60
    total_logged = session1_minutes + session2_minutes
    gap = target_minutes - total_logged
    gap = int(gap) if gap.denominator == 1 else float(gap)
    return gap

print("--- Billable Hours Gap Calculator (Exact Fractions) ---")
print("Enter your daily target and two logged sessions as fractions of an hour.\n")

target  = int(input("Daily billable requirement (hours): "))
n1      = int(input("Morning session — numerator (e.g. 3 for 3/4): "))
d1      = int(input("Morning session — denominator (e.g. 4 for 3/4): "))
n2      = int(input("Afternoon session — numerator (e.g. 7 for 7/10): "))
d2      = int(input("Afternoon session — denominator (e.g. 10 for 7/10): "))

result = billable_gap(target, Fraction(n1, d1), Fraction(n2, d2))
print(f"\nMinutes still needed to meet your billable target: {result}")

Run it in Windows Terminal by typing python, pasting the code, and entering each value when prompted — the final line returns the exact number of minutes still owed for the day.

# Billable Hours Gap Calculator

This skill computes the number of minutes a professional still needs to log in order to reach a daily billable hours target. It takes a target in whole hours and two sessions expressed as fractions of an hour. It converts all values to minutes, sums the logged sessions, and subtracts the total from the target to produce the remaining gap. The result is returned as a whole number of minutes when possible, or as a rounded decimal otherwise.

---

## Dependencies

No external libraries required. For exact fraction arithmetic (zero floating-point error), the `fractions` module from the Python standard library may be used as an optional enhancement.

---

## Inputs

- **Daily billable requirement**: A whole number representing the target hours to bill per day (e.g., 2)
- **Morning session — numerator**: The top number of the fraction representing the first session (e.g., 3 for ¾)
- **Morning session — denominator**: The bottom number of the fraction representing the first session (e.g., 4 for ¾)
- **Afternoon session — numerator**: The top number of the fraction representing the second session (e.g., 7 for ⁷⁄₁₀)
- **Afternoon session — denominator**: The bottom number of the fraction representing the second session (e.g., 10 for ⁷⁄₁₀)

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Greet and Prompt

Print a short title and a brief description so the user knows what the program expects. Prompt the user to enter the five required values one at a time: the daily target in hours, then the numerator and denominator for the morning session, then the numerator and denominator for the afternoon session.

Accept the daily target and session denominators and numerators as whole numbers. If a future version needs fractional targets (e.g., 1.5 hours), accept the target as a decimal instead.

### Step 2 — Convert the Daily Target to Minutes

Multiply the daily target in hours by 60 to obtain the total number of billable minutes required.

### Step 3 — Convert Each Session to Minutes

For each session, divide its numerator by its denominator to obtain the fraction of an hour, then multiply by 60 to convert to minutes.

If exact arithmetic is required (for instance, in a billing or payroll context), represent each session as an exact fraction before multiplying, so no rounding error accumulates.

### Step 4 — Sum the Logged Minutes

Add the two session values in minutes to obtain the total time already logged for the day.

### Step 5 — Compute the Gap

Subtract the total logged minutes from the daily target in minutes. This is the remaining billable gap.

If the result is a whole number, display it without a decimal point. If it is not a whole number, round it to two decimal places.

### Step 6 — Display the Result

Print the final result with a short, friendly message that frames it in business terms. The output should make clear that this number represents minutes still needed to meet the daily billable target — not a raw number in isolation.
