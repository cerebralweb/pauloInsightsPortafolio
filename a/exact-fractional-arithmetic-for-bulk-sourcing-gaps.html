31 AND 1/8 POUNDS: EXACT FRACTIONAL ARITHMETIC FOR BULK SOURCING GAPS

In this article ❧ Procurement decisions in bulk commodity operations depend on running totals — tracking what has arrived, converting across packaging formats, and computing the remaining gap before committing to another purchase order. A single arithmetic error at this stage means over-ordering (wasted cost) or under-ordering (a broken commitment to the wholesale client). This problem illustrates how addition and subtraction of mixed numbers directly models a real sourcing scenario, and how that same logic can be packaged as a reusable Claude skill for any bulk goods context.

The Problem

A coffee roastery needs 100 pounds of green coffee beans to fulfill a wholesale order. A supplier delivers two sacks, each weighing 24¾ pounds, and a second supplier sends one sack weighing 19⅜ pounds.

How many pounds of green coffee beans does the roastery still need to source? (estimate)

  • a) 65 pounds and 7/8
  • b) 31 pounds and 1/8
  • c) 61 pounds
  • d) 28 pounds
Click to Reveal the Answer

Correct Answer: b) 31 and 1/8 pounds ✅

Math Structure Preserved

The problem requires adding two mixed-number deliveries — first multiplying to account for two identical sacks, then adding the second delivery — and subtracting that total from the target weight. The key is converting all fractions to a common denominator (eighths) before operating, so numerators can be combined without distortion.

Converting 100 to 800/8 before subtracting 551/8 is the elegant move: it turns a whole-number target into the same unit as the fractional total, making the gap calculation exact and immediate.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

The following sequence shows how the sourcing gap is derived:

  1. Estimate first (sanity check): Round 24¾ ≈ 25 and drop 19⅜ ≈ 19. Then 2 × 25 = 50; 50 + 19 = 69; 100 − 69 ≈ 31 lb → eliminates options a, c, and d immediately.
  2. Convert first delivery to eighths: 2 × 24¾ = 48 + 12/8 = 48 + 1 4/8 → 49 4/8 lb
  3. Add the two deliveries: 49 4/8 + 19 3/8 = 68 7/8 lb received
  4. Convert target to eighths: 100 = 800/8
  5. Convert total received to an improper fraction: 68 7/8 = (68 × 8 + 7) / 8 = 551/8
  6. Subtract: 800/8 − 551/8 = 249/8
  7. Convert remainder to a mixed number: 249 ÷ 8 = 31 remainder 1 → 31 1/8 lb

Why This Math Matters for the Business Problem

Sourcing managers rarely receive a single bulk shipment that fills an order exactly. They work in running totals — tracking what has arrived, converting units when suppliers use different packaging, and computing the remaining gap before committing to another purchase order. Adding mixed-number weights and subtracting from a target is the arithmetic backbone of that workflow.

Getting it wrong means over-ordering (wasted cost) or under-ordering (a broken commitment to the wholesale client). The estimation step (Step 0) is not just a classroom trick — it is a practical double-check that eliminates three of four answer choices before a single exact calculation is performed, which is precisely how experienced analysts approach multi-step arithmetic under time pressure.

Mathematical Background

This problem exercises addition and subtraction of mixed numbers, a branch of rational arithmetic. The core technique — converting mixed numbers to a common denominator before operating — traces back to medieval Islamic mathematicians, particularly the work of al-Khwārizmī and later al-Uqlīdisī (10th century), who systematized fractional computation for practical trade and measurement.

The key insight they formalized is that fractions can only be added or subtracted when they share a denominator, because the denominator names the unit being counted. Trying to add fourths and eighths directly is like adding meters and inches without converting first. The estimation-first habit demonstrated in Step 0 is also formalized in mathematics education as front-end estimation — a tool that rapidly eliminates wrong answer choices and catches arithmetic slips before they propagate.

# Coffee Roastery — Inventory Gap Calculator

This skill computes the sourcing gap for a bulk commodity order. The user provides a target weight, the number and size of sacks in each delivery (expressed as mixed numbers with a whole-pound part and a fractional part), and the skill returns exactly how many pounds remain to be sourced — expressed as a clean mixed number, not a decimal. It is designed for scenarios where fractional precision matters, such as bulk coffee, grain, flour, or raw materials purchasing.

---

## Dependencies

- fractions.Fraction from the Python standard library — used to perform exact rational arithmetic and avoid floating-point rounding errors. No third-party libraries required.

---

## Inputs

- **Target weight**: The total pounds of the commodity needed to fulfill the order. A whole number (integer).
- **First delivery — number of sacks**: How many sacks arrived in the first shipment. A whole number.
- **First delivery — weight per sack (whole part)**: The whole-pound portion of each sack's weight. A whole number.
- **First delivery — weight per sack (fraction numerator)**: The numerator of the fractional part of each sack's weight.
- **First delivery — weight per sack (fraction denominator)**: The denominator of the fractional part of each sack's weight.
- **Second delivery — weight (whole part)**: The whole-pound portion of the second delivery's weight. A whole number.
- **Second delivery — weight (fraction numerator)**: The numerator of the fractional part of the second delivery's weight.
- **Second delivery — weight (fraction denominator)**: The denominator of the fractional part of the second delivery's weight.

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Collect Inputs

Prompt the user for each of the eight values listed above, one at a time. Use clear, business-friendly prompts that reference the coffee roastery context (e.g., "Target weight needed (whole lb):"). Accept only whole numbers at each prompt.

### Step 2 — Compute First Delivery Weight

Multiply the number of sacks by the per-sack weight. To do this exactly, represent the per-sack weight as a fraction by combining its whole part and fractional part (whole + numerator/denominator), then multiply by the sack count. Keep the result as an exact fraction throughout — do not convert to decimal at any point.

### Step 3 — Compute Second Delivery Weight

Represent the second delivery's weight as an exact fraction by combining its whole part and fractional part (whole + numerator/denominator).

### Step 4 — Compute Total Received

Add the first and second delivery weights. Because both are exact fractions, the addition produces an exact fractional result with no rounding.

### Step 5 — Compute the Sourcing Gap

Subtract the total received from the target weight. Convert the target weight to a fraction before subtracting so the operation stays exact.

### Step 6 — Format and Display the Result

Separate the result into its whole part and its remaining fractional part. If the fractional part is zero, display only the whole number. Otherwise, display the result as a mixed number (e.g., "31 and 1/8 lb"). Print the result with a short, friendly business-context line — for example: "Green coffee beans still to source: 31 and 1/8 lb."
